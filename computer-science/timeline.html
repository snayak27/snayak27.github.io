Tower/Timeline
Nothing was invented in a vacuum

The timeline, cause by cause

Over 180 years of computing history, oldest first. Each milestone is coloured by the floor of the tower it belongs to — and for almost every one, there is a real need that forced it into existence. The army needed firing tables; the phone company needed a reliable switch; the air force and NASA needed computers small enough to fly. Read the "why" lines: that is where the story lives.

Era 1

The Pioneer Era (1843–1956)

From the first mechanical algorithms to the math of universal computers and initial learning machines.

1843

Lovelace's Algorithm — the first computer program

Ada Lovelace translates an article on Charles Babbage's proposed mechanical computer, the Analytical Engine. In her extensive translator notes, she writes the first algorithm designed to be carried out by a machine (to compute Bernoulli numbers), recognizing that computers could manipulate symbols beyond numbers, including music and art.

Why it happened: Babbage was focused purely on mathematical calculation and building the hardware. Lovelace saw the broader potential: if you could mechanize the manipulation of abstract symbols, the machine could execute generalized logic—founding the concept of software. Floor 1
1854

Boolean Algebra — the logic of 1s and 0s

George Boole publishes An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, presenting a mathematical system of logic where variables can only have two values: True (1) or False (0), combined using operations like AND, OR, and NOT.

Why it happened: Boole sought to express the laws of human thought and logical reasoning in a formal algebraic language. This mathematical logic eventually became the foundational design language for all digital switching circuits and computer programs. Floor 1
1890

The Hollerith Tabulator — the birth of automated data

Herman Hollerith designs an electromechanical tabulating machine that uses punched paper cards to record and count census data. The system automatically tallies statistics by running electrical wires through card holes to complete circuits.

Why it happened: the 1880 US Census took nearly a decade to count by hand, and the 1890 census was projected to take even longer. Hollerith built the machine to automate data compilation for the Census Bureau, founding the data processing industry that later became IBM. Floor 2
1936

Turing's Paper Machine — the math that begot code

Alan Turing designs an abstract, mathematical machine that reads and writes symbols on an infinite paper tape. It is the theoretical blueprint for all software and hardware ever made, proving that a single machine could perform any possible calculation.

Why it happened: mathematicians were obsessed with proving whether every math problem could be solved by a step-by-step recipe. To prove some couldn't, Turing first had to invent the mathematical definition of a "recipe" — accidentally inventing the computer. Floor 1
1937

Shannon's Logic Gates — bridging math and switches

Claude Shannon publishes his Master's thesis proving that electric relays and switches can physically implement any Boolean logical operation. By structuring switches in parallel or series, circuits can resolve AND, OR, and NOT gates.

Why it happened: telephone routing networks were designed using ad-hoc switching diagrams that were incredibly complex. Shannon realized that Boole's algebraic logic could optimize these circuits, proving that electricity could calculate logic. Floor 2
1943

McCulloch-Pitts Neuron — computing with biological logic

Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts publish a mathematical model of a simplified biological neuron. They prove that networks of these simple binary threshold units (which turn on or off depending on their inputs) can calculate any logical function that a Turing machine can.

Why it happened: the creators wanted to understand how the human brain produces thoughts and decisions. By mapping biological neurons to binary logic gates, they created the absolute beginning of neural network theory. Floor 6
1944

Colossus — codebreaking in wartime

Tommy Flowers designs Colossus, the world's first programmable electronic digital computer. It uses 1,500 vacuum tubes to perform boolean logic calculations to decipher encrypted telegraph messages.

Why it happened: British codebreakers needed to crack the Lorenz cipher used by the German high command. The messages were too complex to decrypt manually or with electromechanical machines in time, requiring a high-speed electronic solution. Floor 2
1945

ENIAC — electricity replaces human calculators

The world's first general-purpose electronic computer is built: a 30-ton giant containing 17,468 glowing vacuum tubes, computing 5,000 additions per second. It proved that electricity could calculate faster than human brainpower.

Why it happened: World War II artillery crews needed "firing tables" containing thousands of trajectory paths. Human math experts (called "computers") using desk calculators took weeks to finish one. The army needed lightning-fast calculations, so they funded an electronic one. Floor 2
1945

The Stored-Program Concept — putting code in the pantry

John von Neumann outlines the EDVAC design, proposing that a computer's instructions should be stored in the same memory as its data. This meant computers could be reprogrammed in seconds by loading new software rather than physically rewiring the machine.

Why it happened: reprogramming ENIAC was a nightmare. Teams of operators had to spend days flipping switches and replugging massive cables. The solution was simple but revolutionary: store the instructions as numbers, just like data. Floor 3
1947

The Transistor — the solid-state revolution

Bell Labs physicists create the first transistor—a microscopic switch made of solid silicon. It does the same job as a hot, fragile vacuum tube but uses a fraction of the power, generates almost no heat, and never burns out.

Why it happened: the global telephone network was choked by millions of vacuum tubes that died constantly, requiring round-the-clock replacement. Bell Labs needed a reliable, solid switch to handle millions of phone calls without breaking down. Floor 2
1948

Information Theory — measuring the invisible "bit"

Claude Shannon publishes a paper proving that all human communication—text, sound, images—can be boiled down to binary "bits" (1s and 0s) and transmitted perfectly over noisy wires using math.

Why it happened: Bell Labs wanted to calculate the absolute speed limit of sending signals down copper phone lines. Shannon solved it, and in doing so, proved that all media could be digitized. Floor 1
1950

The Turing Test — defining machine intelligence

Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," proposing the "Imitation Game" (now the Turing Test) as a practical answer to the question, "Can machines think?" Instead of arguing definitions, he suggests that if a computer can pass as human in a text conversation, it has achieved intelligence.

Why it happened: early computers were starting to exist, and philosophers were already locked in endless debates about whether a physical box could possess a soul or mind. Turing cut through the philosophy with a functional, conversational benchmark. Floor 6
1952

The First Compiler — teaching computers English

Grace Hopper designs the A-0 compiler system for the UNIVAC I, proving that humans can write code using English-like words which a program then automatically translates into machine numbers.

Why it happened: early programming meant writing endless lists of raw binary math. It was slow, frustrating, and prone to tiny mistakes. Hopper argued that since computers are good at math, they should do the translation work, not humans. Floor 5
1954

LISP — the language of symbolic reasoning

John McCarthy designs LISP (List Processing), introducing functional programming concepts, automatic garbage collection, recursive function logic, and a structure where code and data share the same list format (homoiconicity).

Why it happened: early programming languages like FORTRAN were built strictly for numerical calculations. Artificial Intelligence researchers needed a language that could process symbolic lists of words, concepts, and relationships to model logical thinking. Floor 5
1956

Dartmouth Workshop — naming "Artificial Intelligence"

A summer gathering of brilliant minds coins the term "Artificial Intelligence," launching a new scientific field dedicated to building machines that can learn, reason, and solve problems like humans.

Why it happened: with the first programmable computers finally operational, researchers bet that human thinking was just a set of logical steps that could be coded into a machine. Floor 6
Era 2

The Microchip & Silicon Era (1957–1974)

Shrinking physical gates onto pieces of rock, leading to OS, database, and network standards.

1957

The Mark I Perceptron — the first learning neural network

Frank Rosenblatt builds the Perceptron, a custom analog computer designed for image recognition. It is the first machine that learns weights and associations from data, adjusting its circuits automatically using a mathematical error-correction rule when it makes mistakes.

Why it happened: early AI researchers programmed computers with strict, hand-written logical rules. Rosenblatt believed that true intelligence must be learned dynamically from sensory experience, bypassing human-engineered logic. Floor 6
1957

FORTRAN — software for scientists

IBM releases FORTRAN (Formula Translation), letting engineers write mathematical equations like A = B + C instead of raw machine numbers. It becomes the first widely adopted high-level programming language.

Why it happened: programming was still too slow for busy scientists who needed to solve physics and math equations. IBM needed a language that looked like math, making programmers 10 times more productive. Floor 5
1958–59

The Integrated Circuit — carving circuits in stone

Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce independently find a way to place multiple transistors, resistors, and wires onto a single slice of silicon. It eliminates hand-soldering and births the modern microchip.

Why it happened: the "tyranny of numbers"—to make more powerful computers, engineers had to solder millions of tiny components by hand. A single bad connection ruined the whole machine. Carving everything onto one piece of silicon was the only escape. Floor 2
1959

COBOL — standardizing business software

A joint committee of computer builders and military officers (under Grace Hopper's guidance) defines COBOL (Common Business Oriented Language), which lets programmers write business data processing software in simple, English-like syntax.

Why it happened: early software was custom-tailored for scientific calculations and could only run on one specific model of computer. Companies needed a portable, non-mathematical language they could use across different hardware systems to run billing, databases, and payroll. Floor 5
1962–69

Minuteman & Apollo — funding the chip revolution

The US military and NASA buy integrated circuits in massive quantities to build guidance computers for the Minuteman II missile and the Apollo spacecraft, single-handedly funding the scale-up of chip factories.

Why it happened: missiles and spacecraft couldn't carry 30-ton rooms of vacuum tubes. They needed light, tiny, low-power computers. NASA and the military paid premium prices for chips, driving down manufacturing costs and making chips affordable for civilian use. Floor 2
1964

IBM System/360 — software standardization

IBM launches a family of computers that all share the same instruction set. For the first time, software written for a small computer can run unmodified on a giant mainframe.

Why it happened: before the 360, every computer model had unique hardware. When a business upgraded to a faster machine, they had to throw away all their code and rewrite it from scratch. IBM standardizes hardware to save their customers' software investments. Floor 3
1965

Moore's Law — predicting exponential chip scaling

Gordon Moore publishes a paper predicting that the number of transistors on a single silicon microchip will double roughly every two years, providing more power at lower costs.

Why it happened: the silicon industry was just beginning. Moore wanted to convince businesses that microchips were not a temporary fad, but rather a technology destined to become exponentially cheaper and more powerful over time, driving long-term investment. Floor 2
1967

Simula — the birth of Object-Oriented Programming

Ole-Johan Dahl and Kristen Nygaard release Simula 67, introducing the concepts of classes, objects, inheritance, and subclasses. It organizes software around modular "objects" containing both data and actions rather than just lists of instructions.

Why it happened: programming complex physical systems (like ship routes or factory flows) in traditional sequential code was too complicated and prone to logic errors. Simula's creators wanted a way to model entities that matched real-world behaviors. Floor 5
1968

The Mother of All Demos — glimpsing the future

Douglas Engelbart stuns the tech world by demonstrating the computer mouse, graphical windows, clickable hypertext links, real-time text editing, and live video conferencing in a single 90-minute demo.

Why it happened: in 1968, computers were cold, massive calculators fed on punch cards. Engelbart envisioned computers as collaborative tools that could extend human intelligence, inventing the entire modern user interface in one go. Floor 7
1969

ARPANET — the first node of the internet

Four university computers are linked together, successfully exchanging messages by breaking data into tiny "packets" that find their own way across a network. It is the birth of the Internet.

Why it happened: supercomputers were incredibly expensive. Researchers at different universities needed a way to share access to these rare machines remotely, forcing the creation of a robust, decentralized network. Floor 4
1969–71

Unix — lean, modular operating systems

Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie create Unix, an operating system built on a simple philosophy: write small programs that do one thing well and chain them together. It becomes the software foundation of macOS, Linux, and the cloud.

Why it happened: their previous operating system project, Multics, became over-engineered and bloated. The creators wanted a lean, fun system they could run on a discarded, low-power minicomputer (mostly to play a game called Space Travel). Floor 3
1970

The Relational Database — data in clean tables

Edgar Codd proposes organizing database records into simple rows and columns across linked tables, separating the database's logical structure from its physical storage.

Why it happened: early database programs had to know exactly which disk sectors the data was written to. If you changed the physical layout, all the code broke. Codd wanted a system where developers could query *what* they wanted without worrying about *where* it was stored. Floor 5
1971

The Cook-Levin Theorem — mapping hard math limits

Stephen Cook and Leonid Levin independently prove that the "Boolean Satisfiability Problem" (SAT) is NP-complete. This formalizes the class of NP-complete problems—thousands of incredibly difficult scheduling, routing, and optimization problems that are computationally equivalent.

Why it happened: computer scientists were hitting a wall where certain practical problems (like finding the shortest delivery route) took billions of years to calculate as they grew. Cook and Levin wanted to prove mathematically whether these problems were inherently hard, or if we just hadn't found the right shortcut yet (the P vs NP question). Floor 1
1971

Intel 4004 — the computer on a fingernail

Intel crams an entire Central Processing Unit (~2,300 transistors) onto a single silicon chip. It marks the birth of the microprocessor, packing the power of room-sized machines into a tiny chip.

Why it happened: a Japanese calculator company wanted Intel to build 12 custom, specialized chips for a new desktop calculator. Intel realized designing 12 separate chips was too expensive and proposed building one flexible, programmable chip instead. Floor 2
1972

The C Language — portable systems programming

Dennis Ritchie at Bell Labs designs the C programming language. It combines the close-to-hardware control of assembly language with high-level readability, allowing programmers to write code that can run on completely different computer architectures with minimal changes.

Why it happened: the Unix operating system was originally written in raw assembly code for a specific computer (PDP-7). To rewrite Unix for newer machines, developers needed a high-level language that was powerful enough to talk to hardware but portable enough to compile on any system. Floor 5
1973

Xerox Alto — the first desktop workstation

Xerox PARC builds the Alto, the first computer designed with a mouse, graphical windows, clickable menus, local ethernet networking, and a bitmapped display that shows documents exactly as they will print (WYSIWYG).

Why it happened: computers in the early 1970s were still operated via punch cards or plain text terminal screens. PARC researchers wanted to build an office tool that simulated a physical desk with folders, pages, and trash cans, establishing the GUI model. Floor 7
1973–74

TCP/IP & Ethernet — the universal network language

Bob Metcalfe invents Ethernet to wire computers in the same building, while Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn design TCP/IP—a set of protocols that allows completely different networks to connect and pass packets.

Why it happened: by the early 1970s, different networks (radio, satellite, landlines) existed but couldn't talk to each other. Engineers needed a universal translation protocol so any computer network could communicate with any other—the birth of the inter-net. Floor 4
1974

SQL — standardizing relational queries

Donald Chamberlin and Raymond Boyce design SQL (Structured Query Language) at IBM to access and manipulate data stored in relational databases.

Why it happened: Codd's relational database theory (1970) was a mathematical breakthrough but lacked a standard query language. Developers needed a way to query databases by declaring *what* data they wanted, leaving the database engine to find the most efficient path to retrieve it. Floor 5
Era 3

The Personal & Connected Era (1975–2005)

Computers enter living rooms, networks activate globally, and the web makes it all visual.

1975–77

The Personal Computer — moving to the living room

The Altair 8800 (1975) and the Apple II (1977) launch the home computing era, converting the microprocessor from a corporate tool into a personal appliance for hobbies, games, and writing.

Why it happened: the cheap microprocessors created in the early 1970s made parts inexpensive enough that hobbyists could build their own computers. The drop in hardware cost created an entirely new type of user: the general public. Floor 7
1977

Public-Key Cryptography (RSA) — trusting strangers

Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman invent RSA cryptography, using prime numbers to create public and private key pairs. Anyone can lock a message with the public key, but only the recipient can unlock it with their private key.

Why it happened: as computer networks began connecting unrelated organizations and people, sharing a secret password (symmetric key) securely over the wire became impossible. Trusting networks required a way to send secure messages without exchanging a password first. Floor 1 → Floor 4
1981

MS-DOS & the IBM PC — standardizing the desktop

Microsoft releases MS-DOS to power the newly launched IBM Personal Computer. Rather than custom-building software for unique machine hardware, MS-DOS provides a standard operating system layer that lets software developers write applications for any IBM-compatible PC.

Why it happened: IBM rushed to build a personal computer to compete with Apple, using off-the-shelf hardware parts. They contracted Microsoft to provide the operating system, creating a software standard that unified the office desktop computer market. Floor 3
1983

The Internet Activates & DNS is Born

On January 1, 1983, the ARPANET officially switches to TCP/IP. In the same year, the Domain Name System (DNS) is deployed, translating memorable names like example.com into numeric IP addresses.

Why it happened: the network was outgrowing its centralized list of names (a single HOSTS.TXT file copied by hand by every computer). DNS was needed to delegate address lookups to thousands of distributed servers, allowing the internet to scale infinitely. Floor 4
1984

The Macintosh — computers "for the rest of us"

Apple releases the Macintosh, bringing the graphical user interface (GUI)—featuring a mouse, desktop folders, icons, and menus—out of research labs and into the consumer market.

Why it happened: command-line systems required users to memorize cryptic text commands, which scared away ordinary people. To make computers household objects, Apple built an interface that could be operated by pointing and clicking at visual metaphors. Floor 7
1986

Backpropagation — training deep neural networks

Geoffrey Hinton, David Rumelhart, and Ronald Williams publish a paper demonstrating that the backpropagation algorithm can train multi-layer neural networks to learn internal representations of data. This allows networks to solve non-linear problems that single-layer perceptrons couldn't.

Why it happened: in the late 1960s, AI fell into a "winter" when math proofs showed that simple neural networks couldn't solve basic logic functions like XOR. Researchers needed a reliable way to distribute errors backward through multiple layers of neurons to adjust weights. Floor 6
1988

The Morris Worm — network security awakens

Robert Tappan Morris releases the first self-replicating computer worm onto the internet. Within hours, a software bug causes the program to repeatedly infect hosts, clogging mail systems and temporarily crashing 10% of all connected Unix computers.

Why it happened: Morris wanted to write a program to gauge the size of the early internet. He exploited vulnerabilities in Unix network protocols and weak passwords, proving that networks needed security models and dedicated incident response teams (CERT). Floor 4
1989–91

The World Wide Web — a web of documents

Tim Berners-Lee invents HTTP, HTML, and URLs at CERN, creating an open system of hyperlinked documents that sit on top of the physical infrastructure of the internet.

Why it happened: CERN scientists had documents scattered across hundreds of incompatible computers. Finding research papers required logging into separate systems with unique logins. Berners-Lee wanted a single, universal way to link information across all platforms. Floor 4
1991

Python Language — code as readable as prose

Guido van Rossum releases Python, a language that uses whitespace indentation to structure blocks of code. It prioritizes programmer productivity, code readability, and simplicity, eventually becoming the dominant language for scripting, data analysis, and modern AI development.

Why it happened: Van Rossum was frustrated by the complex syntax and manual memory management of languages like C and C++. He wanted a hobby project over Christmas to build an intuitive, readable scripting language that was still powerful enough for system administration. Floor 5
1991

Linux — open source conquers the servers

Linus Torvalds releases the Linux kernel as a free, open-source operating system project. It grows to become the operating system that runs the modern cloud, Android phones, and supercomputers.

Why it happened: Unix was the industry-standard OS but was locked behind expensive corporate licenses. Students and hobbyists wanted a free, Unix-compatible kernel that they could read, modify, and run on cheap consumer PCs. Floor 3
1993

Mosaic — the web gets graphics

The NCSA releases Mosaic, the first popular web browser that displays images directly inline with text inside a single window, sparking the 1990s dot-com boom.

Why it happened: the early web was plain text and required opening images in separate windows. To appeal to a mass audience, the web needed to look like a magazine page—unifying text, layout, and pictures in one place. Floor 7
1995

Java & JavaScript — interactive web pages

Sun Microsystems launches Java, enabling cross-platform server code, and Brendan Eich writes JavaScript for Netscape, embedding a scripting engine directly inside the web browser.

Why it happened: early websites were static, dead documents. To build dynamic web applications (like search engines, shopping carts, and interactive games), the browser needed to run its own code in real-time on the user's computer. Floor 7
1997

Deep Blue defeats Kasparov — brute-force mastery

IBM's Deep Blue supercomputer defeats world chess champion Garry Kasparov in a six-game match under standard time controls. It uses custom parallel processor hardware to search up to 200 million board positions per second.

Why it happened: researchers wanted to see if a machine could conquer what was then considered the ultimate benchmark of human logical intellect. Deep Blue proved that parallel computing, combined with hand-coded heuristic search algorithms, could overpower human strategic intuition in structured environments. Floor 6
1998

PageRank — mapping web relevance

Larry Page and Sergey Brin launch Google using PageRank, an algorithm that judges a web page's importance by counting the number and quality of other pages linking to it.

Why it happened: the web was growing by millions of pages a year. Search engines of the time indexed keywords stupidly, leading to spam. Users needed an algorithm that understood *relevance* and trust to find quality information. Floor 5
1999

GeForce 256 — birth of the GPU

NVIDIA releases the GeForce 256, coining the term "Graphics Processing Unit" (GPU). It integrates hardware transform and lighting engines directly onto the graphics card, offloading heavy 3D geometry calculations from the CPU.

Why it happened: real-time 3D games were becoming popular, but rendering millions of polygons choked the general-purpose CPU. The industry needed specialized, highly parallel processors dedicated purely to rendering pixels and triangles. This architecture would later become the hardware engine of modern AI. Floor 2
2004

MapReduce — parallel big data processing

Google engineers publish the MapReduce programming model, introducing a framework that automatically splits massive data sorting and indexing computations across clusters of thousands of cheap, commodity servers.

Why it happened: the World Wide Web was expanding exponentially, and single database systems could no longer scale to index it. Developers needed a software layer that could distribute calculations and handle server hardware failures automatically. Floor 5
2005

Git — decentralized software collaboration

Linus Torvalds invents Git, a decentralized version control system. It allows thousands of developers to work on the same codebase concurrently, merging changes asynchronously without depending on a single central server.

Why it happened: the Linux kernel development team lost their free license for the proprietary version control system BitKeeper. Frustrated by the limitations of existing open-source options (like CVS and SVN) which were slow and central-server bound, Torvalds spent two weeks writing Git from scratch to handle distributed workflows. Floor 5
Era 4

The Mobile & Cloud Era (2006–2016)

Renting elastic scale in the cloud, pocket-sized touch screens, and the neural network renaissance.

2006

AWS — computing becomes a utility

Amazon launches Web Services (AWS), letting developers rent computing power, storage, and databases over the internet on a pay-as-you-go basis.

Why it happened: web startups had to spend thousands buying physical servers that sat idle 90% of the time, only to crash if they went viral. Renting elastic capacity on-demand turned infrastructure from a barrier to a utility. Floor 4
2007

The iPhone — pocket-sized mobile computing

Apple launches the iPhone, combining multi-touch screens, mobile internet, and a desktop-grade web browser into a pocket-sized computer.

Why it happened: early smartphones were clunky devices with tiny keys and crippled web browsers. Using the web on the move was frustrating; people needed a device that behaved like a real computer but could be controlled entirely by finger touch. Floor 7
2007

CUDA — the GPU becomes a math accelerator

NVIDIA launches CUDA, a programming model that allows developers to run general-purpose calculations on a GPU's thousands of tiny cores, not just graphics.

Why it happened: scientists noticed that graphics cards were exceptionally fast at matrix mathematics but could only be programmed using graphics code (like textures and shaders). They needed a clean way to run mathematical calculations directly on GPUs. Floor 3
2008

Bitcoin & Blockchain — decentralized trustless ledger

Satoshi Nakamoto publishes the Bitcoin whitepaper, combining peer-to-peer networking, public-key cryptography, and a proof-of-work consensus algorithm to create the first decentralized, trustless database ledger.

Why it happened: traditional digital transaction systems required a trusted third party (like a bank) to prevent double-spending. Nakamoto wanted to create a digital currency that could be transferred directly between users without any central institution, relying instead on cryptographic consensus. Floor 5
2009

ImageNet — scaling AI training data

Fei-Fei Li and her Stanford team release ImageNet, a massive database of over 14 million hand-annotated images organized into 20,000 categories. It establishes a benchmark dataset for training and testing computer vision models.

Why it happened: for decades, neural network algorithms were bottlenecked because they were trained on small, clean datasets. ImageNet proved that scaling training data to millions of real-world examples was the key to unlocking deep learning performance. Floor 5
2010

Rust Language — compile-time memory safety

Graydon Hoare and Mozilla announce Rust. By introducing the concept of "ownership" and "borrowing" checked strictly at compile time, it achieves the raw performance and low-level control of C/C++ without requiring a garbage collector, preventing common bugs like null pointer dereferences and buffer overflows.

Why it happened: web browsers (like Firefox) written in C++ were plagued by security vulnerabilities and stability issues caused by manual memory management. Developers needed a language that was fast enough for system-level programming but guaranteed memory safety automatically. Floor 5
2012

AlexNet — the rebirth of deep learning

Alex Krizhevsky and Geoffrey Hinton train a deep convolutional neural network on GPUs to win the ImageNet computer vision competition, beating traditional methods by a massive margin.

Why it happened: for decades, neural networks were dismissed as impractical. AlexNet proved that combining millions of labeled images (ImageNet) with massively parallel graphics processors (via CUDA) could make neural networks incredibly smart. Floor 6
2014

GANs — competitive deep learning

Ian Goodfellow and his colleagues introduce Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), setting two neural networks to compete in a game: a Generator creates synthetic images while a Discriminator tries to catch the fakes.

Why it happened: deep learning models were great at classifying or recognizing existing patterns, but they struggled to generate new, realistic data. GANs created a self-improving training loop that unlocked deep generative media. Floor 6
2015

Kubernetes — the cloud operating system

Google open-sources Kubernetes, an orchestration system that automates the deployment, scaling, and management of containerized applications across clusters of physical server hardware.

Why it happened: as web applications transitioned to microservices running in isolated containers (Docker), managing thousands of containers manually was impossible. Developers needed a cloud-level operating system to automate load balancing, hardware failures, and scheduling. Floor 3
2016

AlphaGo — mastering intuitive strategy

DeepMind's AlphaGo program defeats legendary Go champion Lee Sedol 4–1 in Seoul, South Korea, using deep neural networks and reinforcement learning to evaluate board positions using human-like "intuition."

Why it happened: unlike chess, the game of Go has more board configurations than there are atoms in the observable universe. Brute-force calculations were useless; AI needed to learn how to evaluate board configurations by "feel" and pattern appreciation, just like human masters. Floor 6
Era 5

The Generative AI Era (2017–2026)

Transformers parallelize sequences, enabling massive language scale and local silicon accelerations.

2017

★ The Transformer — parallelizing language

Google researchers publish "Attention Is All You Need," introducing the Transformer architecture. By reading sentences all at once rather than word-by-word, it enables massive scale and forms the basis of all modern LLMs.

Why it happened: earlier language models (RNNs) analyzed text sequentially. They were slow to train on GPUs and forgot context over long articles. Researchers needed an architecture that could read entire blocks of text in parallel and pay "attention" to related words. Why it's the turn: attention is just linear algebra done in parallel — a change on Floor 1, the mathematics. Because every floor stands on Floor 1, that one change rippled up the whole tower. 2018–20

Scaling Up — the rise of large language models

AI labs scale up the Transformer architecture, training massive models on internet-scale text. GPT-3 (2020) demonstrates that scaling size alone leads to unexpected capabilities like writing essays and code.

Why it happened: because the Transformer could be trained in parallel on GPUs, developers realized they could feed it the entire internet if they made the model big enough. The result was a sudden jump in linguistic fluency. Floor 6
2021

GitHub Copilot — AI pair programming

GitHub and OpenAI launch Copilot, the first widely adopted AI coding assistant. It uses a specialized Large Language Model (Codex) integrated directly inside developer text editors to suggest code lines and functions in real-time.

Why it happened: Large Language Models were powerful but isolated in web sandboxes. To be useful, they needed to be embedded directly into human workflows, proving that generative AI could act as a collaborative pair programmer. Floor 7
2022

ChatGPT — conversational interfaces for AI

OpenAI wraps a chat interface around GPT-3.5, launching ChatGPT. It becomes the fastest-adopted consumer application in history, reaching 100 million active users in two months.

Why it happened: the raw language models were already powerful but required complex prompting techniques. Wrapping them in a simple, familiar chat box democratized access, allowing anyone to chat with the AI naturally. Floor 7
2024

Agentic AI & MCP — software gets its hands

Anthropic open-sources the Model Context Protocol (MCP), establishing a universal standard for AI models to connect securely to local files, databases, IDEs, and APIs. AI transitions from a passive text box to an active agent that can write code, run commands, and execute complex workflows.

Why it happened: Large Language Models were isolated in sandboxes, unable to interact with the real world. To do practical work, they needed a standard way to interface with tools and data, similar to how USB standardized hardware connections. Floor 6
2025

DGX Blackwell — local supercomputing

NVIDIA ships Blackwell-based local supercomputers, bringing data-center-grade neural network processing directly to developer desks.

Why it happened: renting massive AI clusters in the cloud was becoming expensive, and enterprises worried about data privacy. Developers needed a way to run and fine-tune large models locally, pushing the hardware floor to pack more power into desktop form factors. Floor 2
2025–26

Reasoning Models — inference-time compute scaling

AI labs transition from next-token prediction to reinforcement-learning-driven reasoning models (like OpenAI's o1/o3 and DeepSeek-R1). These models use inference-time compute to run a chain of thought, self-correct, and search for solutions before outputting a response.

Why it happened: pre-training web data was reaching scaling limits, and standard LLMs struggled with complex mathematics, code synthesis, and logical reasoning. Shifting compute scaling from the training phase (next-token prediction) to the inference phase (searching and thinking) unlocked human-level performance on academic benchmarks. Floor 6
