Tower/Timeline
Nothing was invented in a vacuum

The timeline, cause by cause

Over 180 years of computing history, oldest first. Each milestone is coloured by the floor of the tower it belongs to — and for almost every one, there is a real need that forced it into existence. The army needed firing tables; the phone company needed a reliable switch; the air force and NASA needed computers small enough to fly. Read the "why" lines: that is where the story lives.

Era 1

The Pioneer Era (1843–1956)

From the first mechanical algorithms to the math of universal computers and initial learning machines.

1843

Lovelace's Algorithm — the first computer program

Ada Lovelace writes the first algorithm designed to be carried out by a machine, recognizing that computers could manipulate symbols beyond numbers, including music and art.

🔴 What it changed: Before Lovelace, mechanical calculators were seen strictly as number crunchers. She realized that if a machine could manipulate numbers, those numbers could represent music, art, or language.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 5 (Data & Software). Birthed the concept of software 100 years before physical computers could run it.
📱 Use Today: It is why your phone doesn't just do math, but displays photos, plays music, and streams video.
Floor 1
1854

Boolean Algebra — the logic of 1s and 0s

George Boole publishes a mathematical system of logic where variables can only have two values: True (1) or False (0), combined using operations like AND, OR, and NOT.

🔴 What it changed: Logical reasoning was a branch of philosophy. Boole turned it into exact algebra—proving logic could be calculated like math.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Provided the mathematical framework that Shannon later used to design electrical logic gates.
📱 Use Today: Every database query, search filter, and chip transaction runs on Boolean logic.
Floor 1
1890

The Hollerith Tabulator — the birth of automated data

Herman Hollerith designs an electromechanical tabulating machine that uses punched paper cards to record and count census data.

🔴 What it changed: Counting data was done entirely by hand. Hollerith used electrical wires running through card holes to automate counting, reducing tabulating time from years to months.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 5 (Data). Birthed electromechanical hardware that later evolved into digital storage.
📱 Use Today: Hollertih's company merged to become IBM, founding the enterprise data processing industry.
Floor 2
1936

Turing's Paper Machine — the math that begot code

Alan Turing designs an abstract mathematical machine that reads and writes symbols on tape, proving a single machine could perform any possible calculation.

🔴 What it changed: People believed each task required a custom calculator (one machine for division, one for sorting). Turing proved a single machine could run any possible program.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 3 (Architecture) & Floor 5 (Software). Defined the general-purpose computer and separated software from hardware.
📱 Use Today: Why your smartphone can be a camera, GPS, or a game console simply by loading different apps.
Floor 1
1937

Shannon's Logic Gates — bridging math and switches

Claude Shannon proves that electric relays and switches can physically implement Boolean logical operations, establishing digital circuit design.

🔴 What it changed: Switching networks for telephone routing were designed by trial-and-error. Shannon proved that electrical switches could calculate Boolean logic.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Translated abstract logic into physical, silicon-based computer hardware.
📱 Use Today: The basic architecture of every silicon processor in every computer, router, and smartphone on Earth.
Floor 2
1943

McCulloch-Pitts Neuron — computing with biological logic

Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts publish a mathematical model of a simplified biological neuron, showing it can calculate any logical function.

🔴 What it changed: Biology and logic circuits were studied separately. They proved that simplified networks of binary neurons could compute any mathematical function.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI). The mathematical birth of artificial neural networks, modeled on biological brains.
📱 Use Today: The absolute ancestral math behind modern neural networks, deep learning, and AI.
Floor 6
1944

Colossus — codebreaking in wartime

Tommy Flowers designs Colossus, using vacuum tubes to decipher encrypted telegraph messages at electronic speeds.

🔴 What it changed: Codebreaking was done by hand or mechanical machines. Colossus used vacuum tubes to calculate logical combinations at electronic speeds.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Proved that vacuum tubes could build reliable, high-speed electronic logic machines.
📱 Use Today: Birthed the electronic digital computer age, proving that physical hardware could replace mechanical switches.
Floor 2
1945

ENIAC — electricity replaces human calculators

The world's first general-purpose electronic computer is built: a 30-ton giant containing 17,468 glowing vacuum tubes, computing 5,000 additions per second.

🔴 What it changed: Ballistic calculations took weeks for human 'computers' using mechanical calculators. ENIAC calculated them in seconds using purely electronic vacuum tubes.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). The world's first general-purpose electronic digital computer, proving the feasibility of large-scale computing.
📱 Use Today: The starting point of all electronic computing hardware in human history.
Floor 2
1945

The Stored-Program Concept — putting code in the pantry

John von Neumann proposes storing program instructions in the same memory as data, allowing the machine to be reprogrammed in seconds.

🔴 What it changed: Reprogramming a computer meant spending days physically rewiring cables and switches. Storing instructions as data made computers instantly reprogrammable.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Turned computers from hardwired calculators into dynamically programmable processors.
📱 Use Today: Why you can switch apps instantly or download new software without rebuilding your phone.
Floor 3
1947

The Transistor — the solid-state revolution

Bell Labs physicists create the first transistor, a solid-state switch made of silicon that replaces hot, fragile vacuum tubes.

🔴 What it changed: Hot, fragile vacuum tubes broke constantly and used massive power. Transistors used solid-state silicon switches that never burned out and used microscopic power.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Allowed computers to shrink from the size of a room to the size of a chip.
📱 Use Today: Billions of transistors fit on a single fingernail-sized processor, powering every modern device.
Floor 2
1948

Information Theory — measuring the invisible "bit"

Claude Shannon proves that all human communication can be boiled down to binary 'bits' (1s and 0s) and transmitted perfectly over noisy wires.

🔴 What it changed: Communications engineers designed radios and phone lines by trial-and-error. Shannon proved all media could be digitized into 'bits' and mathematically defined the speed limit of data transmission.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 4 (Networking). Laid the mathematical foundation for digitizing all human data.
📱 Use Today: Powers every video stream, MP3, 5G signal, and optical fiber connection on Earth.
Floor 1
1950

The Turing Test — defining machine intelligence

Alan Turing proposes the 'Imitation Game' as a practical benchmark to answer the question, 'Can machines think?'

🔴 What it changed: Philosophers argued endlessly over whether a machine could have a 'soul'. Turing replaced the debate with a practical conversational test.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI) & Floor 7 (Humans). Established conversation as the benchmark of artificial intelligence.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark we still use to evaluate models like ChatGPT and Claude.
Floor 6
1952

The First Compiler — teaching computers English

Grace Hopper designs a system that lets humans write code in English-like words, which the program automatically translates into binary.

🔴 What it changed: Programming required writing endless lists of raw binary codes. Grace Hopper designed a program that translated English-like commands into machine code automatically.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Birthed the concept of high-level programming languages.
📱 Use Today: Every line of code written by modern software developers is compiled using Grace Hopper's core concept.
Floor 5
1954

LISP — the language of symbolic reasoning

John McCarthy designs LISP, introducing functional programming, automatic garbage collection, and recursive function logic.

🔴 What it changed: Early programming languages like FORTRAN were built strictly for numerical calculations. LISP processed symbols, concepts, and lists of words to model logical thinking.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 6 (AI). The language that defined symbolic AI reasoning for decades.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered features like garbage collection and dynamic typing used in Python, JavaScript, and Java.
Floor 5
1956

Dartmouth Workshop — naming "Artificial Intelligence"

A summer gathering of researchers coins the term 'Artificial Intelligence', establishing it as a new scientific field.

🔴 What it changed: Separated researchers worked on 'cybernetics' and 'automata'. The workshop united them under the name 'Artificial Intelligence'.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI). Established AI as a distinct academic and engineering discipline.
📱 Use Today: Launched the field of research that created all modern AI tools.
Floor 6
Era 2

The Microchip & Silicon Era (1957–1974)

Shrinking physical gates onto pieces of rock, leading to OS, database, and network standards.

1957

The Mark I Perceptron — the first learning neural network

Frank Rosenblatt builds the Perceptron, the first neural network machine that learns weights and associations from data.

🔴 What it changed: Early AI researchers hand-coded logical rules into machines. The Perceptron learned dynamically from data by adjusting its weights when it made mistakes.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Birthed the field of neural networks that learn from data rather than rules.
📱 Use Today: The conceptual ancestor of all neural network training algorithms.
Floor 6
1957

FORTRAN — software for scientists

IBM releases FORTRAN (Formula Translation), letting engineers write mathematical equations like A = B + C instead of raw machine numbers.

🔴 What it changed: Programming required writing raw numbers for CPU instructions. FORTRAN let programmers write math-like formulas, boosting developer productivity ten-fold.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Established compiler technology that maps high-level human logic to physical CPU instructions.
📱 Use Today: Modern scientific and engineering software still compiles using derivatives of FORTRAN's optimized math compilers.
Floor 5
1958–59

The Integrated Circuit — carving circuits in stone

Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce independently find a way to place multiple transistors, resistors, and wires onto a single slice of silicon.

🔴 What it changed: Building a computer required soldering thousands of tiny parts by hand. A single bad solder joint ruined the machine. Carving components onto one silicon block solved this 'tyranny of numbers'.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Transformed electronic manufacturing from custom assembly to mass-produced printing.
📱 Use Today: The reason every microchip in your phone and computer can contain billions of transistors in a tiny space.
Floor 2
1959

COBOL — standardizing business software

A joint committee defines COBOL, letting programmers write business software in simple, English-like syntax.

🔴 What it changed: Software was custom-tailored for scientific math and ran on only one computer model. COBOL created a portable, business-oriented language for billing and payroll.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 7 (Humans/Apps). Allowed companies to run software across different hardware manufacturers.
📱 Use Today: Still runs behind the scenes in many banking, credit card, and government systems worldwide.
Floor 5
1962–69

Minuteman & Apollo — funding the chip revolution

The US military and NASA buy integrated circuits in massive quantities, single-handedly funding the scale-up of chip factories.

🔴 What it changed: Early silicon chips were custom-built, unreliable, and cost thousands of dollars. NASA and the military bought them in bulk for guidance computers, driving down costs and forcing production standards.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 7 (Apps). Funded the scale-up of silicon factories, making microchips cheap enough for consumer products.
📱 Use Today: Without this government scale-up, microchips would have remained too expensive for personal computers and smartphones.
Floor 2
1964

IBM System/360 — software standardization

IBM launches a family of computers that all share the same instruction set, allowing software to run unmodified across different sizes of mainframes.

🔴 What it changed: Every computer model had unique hardware. Upgrading machines meant throwing away all code and rewriting it from scratch. IBM standardizes hardware interfaces.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Decoupled the software layer from physical machine scale.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered the concept of backwards compatibility that allows old programs to run on modern computers.
Floor 3
1965

Moore's Law — predicting exponential chip scaling

Gordon Moore predicts that the number of transistors on a single silicon microchip will double roughly every two years.

🔴 What it changed: The silicon industry was in its infancy and seen as risky. Moore's prediction set an exponential development roadmap that guided corporate investments.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Became a self-fulfilling prophecy that drove the rapid scaling of hardware performance.
📱 Use Today: The reason computers have consistently become faster, cheaper, and smaller every single year for decades.
Floor 2
1967

Simula — the birth of Object-Oriented Programming

Ole-Johan Dahl and Kristen Nygaard release Simula, introducing the concepts of classes, objects, and inheritance to model physical systems.

🔴 What it changed: Programs were written as sequential lists of instructions. Simula grouped data and actions into modular 'objects' that behave like real-world entities.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Established Object-Oriented Programming (OOP), structuring how software architectures are designed.
📱 Use Today: The paradigm that organizes modern programming languages like Java, C++, Python, and C#.
Floor 5
1968

The Mother of All Demos — glimpsing the future

Douglas Engelbart demonstrates the computer mouse, graphical windows, clickable hypertext, real-time text editing, and video conferencing in a single 90-minute demo.

🔴 What it changed: Computers were cold, massive calculators fed on punch cards. Engelbart demonstrated that they could be collaborative, visual tools to extend human intellect.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Outlined the visual interface concepts that computers spent the next 30 years building.
📱 Use Today: Every element of your desktop interface—mouse, windows, links, and video calls—was shown in this single 1968 demo.
Floor 7
1969

ARPANET — the first node of the internet

Four university computers are linked together, successfully exchanging messages by breaking data into packets that find their own way across a network.

🔴 What it changed: Computer networks relied on dedicated, fragile lines (like phone lines). If one line broke, the connection died. ARPANET introduced packet switching to route data dynamically.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Converted computing from standalone hardware into a connected mesh.
📱 Use Today: The foundational birth of the network design that became the global internet.
Floor 4
1969–71

Unix — lean, modular operating systems

Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie create Unix, an operating system built on a simple philosophy: write small programs that do one thing well and chain them together.

🔴 What it changed: Operating systems were massive, bloated, and over-engineered. Unix proved that a simple, modular system running on cheap minicomputers was more powerful and easier to maintain.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Created a clean system interface (the command line and files) for programmers to build tools.
📱 Use Today: The design foundation for macOS, Linux, Android, and the cloud servers running the internet.
Floor 3
1970

The Relational Database — data in clean tables

Edgar Codd proposes organizing database records into simple rows and columns across linked tables, separating the database's logical structure from physical storage.

🔴 What it changed: Database programs had to know exactly which disk sectors the data was written to. If you changed the physical layout, all the code broke. Codd separated logical tables from physical sectors.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Decoupled data query logic from physical storage hardware.
📱 Use Today: Every transactional database, SQL server, and user registry uses Codd's relational model.
Floor 5
1971

The Cook-Levin Theorem — mapping hard math limits

Stephen Cook and Leonid Levin formalize the class of NP-complete problems—thousands of incredibly difficult scheduling, routing, and optimization problems.

🔴 What it changed: Computer scientists hit a wall where certain practical problems (like routing) took billions of years to calculate, without knowing if they were inherently hard or if we just lacked the right algorithm.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory). Mapped the absolute mathematical boundaries of what computers can solve efficiently.
📱 Use Today: Used by software engineers to identify when a problem is mathematically too hard, prompting them to search for a close approximation instead of a perfect answer.
Floor 1
1971

Intel 4004 — the computer on a fingernail

Intel crams an entire Central Processing Unit (~2,300 transistors) onto a single silicon chip, marking the birth of the microprocessor.

🔴 What it changed: Computers required multiple chips for their central processor. The 4004 consolidated processing logic onto a single, programmable chip.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Consolidated CPU logic onto a single programmable silicon wafer.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered the single-chip CPU architecture that runs inside every phone, laptop, and smart appliance today.
Floor 2
1972

The C Language — portable systems programming

Dennis Ritchie designs the C programming language, combining the close-to-hardware control of assembly with high-level readability and portability.

🔴 What it changed: Operating systems had to be custom-written in raw assembly code for each machine. C allowed developers to write code that could compile and run on different hardware architectures with minimal changes.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Allowed operating systems (like Unix) to be written in a portable, high-level language.
📱 Use Today: Virtually all operating system kernels, device drivers, and game engines are still written in C or C++.
Floor 5
1973

Xerox Alto — the first desktop workstation

Xerox PARC builds the Alto, the first computer designed with a mouse, graphical windows, clickable menus, local ethernet, and a bitmapped display.

🔴 What it changed: Computers were text-only machines operated by command lines. The Alto visualised files as folders, pages as documents, and deleted items as a trash can.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Translated computer directories into a spatial, visual desk metaphor.
📱 Use Today: Defined the graphical interface style that Apple and Windows copied to make computers accessible to the public.
Floor 7
1973–74

TCP/IP & Ethernet — the universal network language

Bob Metcalfe invents Ethernet for local networks, while Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn design TCP/IP to route data across completely different networks.

🔴 What it changed: Different networks were isolated islands. TCP/IP acted as a universal translator, routing packets across different physical mediums (radio, satellite, copper).
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 4 (Networking). Abstractly isolated the physical transmission medium from the data being sent.
📱 Use Today: The backbone protocol of the global internet. Every web page download or video call you make is routed via TCP/IP.
Floor 4
1974

SQL — standardizing relational queries

Donald Chamberlin and Raymond Boyce design SQL at IBM to access and manipulate data stored in relational databases.

🔴 What it changed: Querying database tables required writing complex, custom traversal code. SQL let developers query databases by declaring *what* data they wanted in plain English-like statements.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software). Standardized how databases are queried, creating a clean interface between applications and storage.
📱 Use Today: The universal language for data queries. Every time you log in, search a product, or load a feed, a SQL query runs behind the scenes.
Floor 5
Era 3

The Personal & Connected Era (1975–2005)

Computers enter living rooms, networks activate globally, and the web makes it all visual.

1975–77

The Personal Computer — moving to the living room

The Altair 8800 and the Apple II launch the home computing era, converting the microprocessor into a personal appliance.

🔴 What it changed: Computers were massive corporate and military mainframes. Cheap microprocessors made computers small and cheap enough for hobbyists to buy for their living rooms.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Shifted computer design from industrial scaling to consumer mass-market electronics.
📱 Use Today: Birthed the personal computing revolution, leading to the laptops and tablets we use every day.
Floor 7
1977

Public-Key Cryptography (RSA) — trusting strangers

RSA cryptography uses prime numbers to create public and private key pairs, allowing secure data exchange over insecure lines without sharing a secret password first.

🔴 What it changed: Securing messages required both parties to exchange a secret password (symmetric key) in person. RSA allowed secure communication between total strangers over open wires.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 4 (Networking). Resolved the fundamental trust problem of open networks.
📱 Use Today: Every secure website you visit (indicated by the lock icon and HTTPS) uses public-key cryptography to secure your passwords and credit cards.
Floor 1 → Floor 4
1981

MS-DOS & the IBM PC — standardizing the desktop

Microsoft releases MS-DOS to power the IBM Personal Computer, standardizing the operating system layer for compatible desktop PCs.

🔴 What it changed: Software developers had to custom-tailored programs for each machine model's unique hardware. MS-DOS provided a standard operating system layer, allowing any IBM-compatible PC to run the same software.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Created a unified operating system standard for desktop computing.
📱 Use Today: Standardized the PC industry, launching Microsoft's software monopoly and defining the office desktop computing landscape.
Floor 3
1983

The Internet Activates & DNS is Born

The ARPANET switches to TCP/IP on January 1, 1983, and the Domain Name System (DNS) is deployed to translate memorable names into numeric IP addresses.

🔴 What it changed: The early internet relied on a single text file (HOSTS.TXT) copied by hand to map names to numbers. DNS delegated lookups to a distributed directory system, allowing the internet to scale.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking). Birthed a decentralized phonebook for the web, turning a military prototype into a global utility.
📱 Use Today: Whenever you type a URL like `google.com`, DNS translates it into an IP address in milliseconds behind the scenes.
Floor 4
1983

GNU Project — the free software foundation

Richard Stallman launches the GNU Project to write a complete Unix-compatible operating system that is free for anyone to use, share, and modify.

🔴 What it changed: Commercial Unix vendors locked their source code behind proprietary licenses, preventing programmers from modifying software and sharing bug fixes. GNU introduced 'copyleft' licensing.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Birthed the legal and ethical framework for the open-source software movement.
📱 Use Today: The basis of open-source software licensing (GPL) that powers Linux, the cloud, and modern software development.
Floor 3
1984

The Macintosh — computers "for the rest of us"

Apple launches the Macintosh, the first commercially successful personal computer with a mouse-driven graphical interface.

🔴 What it changed: Personal computers were still text-driven terminal screens. The Macintosh brought graphical windows, folders, and fonts to the mainstream consumer.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Successfully commercialized the desktop metaphor pioneered at Xerox PARC.
📱 Use Today: Established the standard graphical user interface (GUI) experience that every computer and laptop uses today.
Floor 7
1986

Backpropagation — training deep neural networks

Geoffrey Hinton and colleagues popularize backpropagation, a mathematical algorithm for training deep neural networks by propagating errors backwards through layers.

🔴 What it changed: Neural networks were stuck because developers didn't know how to train networks with multiple hidden layers of connections. Backpropagation solved this mathematically.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Provided the mathematical foundation for training deep neural networks.
📱 Use Today: The core optimization algorithm used to train every modern deep learning model and AI system.
Floor 6
1988

The Morris Worm — network security awakens

Robert Morris releases the first major internet worm, exploiting vulnerabilities in Unix networks and shutting down 10% of the connected web.

🔴 What it changed: The early internet was an academic circle built on total trust, with no security firewalls or passwords. The worm proved that networks were highly vulnerable.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Forced the creation of the computer security industry (CERT, firewalls, and encryption).
📱 Use Today: The event that ended the era of trust on the web, forcing the implementation of modern cybersecurity systems.
Floor 4
1989–91

The World Wide Web — a web of documents

Tim Berners-Lee designs HTML, HTTP, and URLs at CERN, creating a decentralized web of documents linked across the internet.

🔴 What it changed: The internet was a collection of command-line tools (email, FTP) that required technical training. The WWW created a visual web of linked pages that anyone could navigate.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 7 (Humans/Apps). Wrapped the internet's protocols in a visual, linked document layer.
📱 Use Today: The foundation of the modern web. Every website you open in your browser runs on Berners-Lee's protocols.
Floor 4
1991

Python Language — code as readable as prose

Guido van Rossum releases Python, emphasizing code readability and clean syntax to make software development faster and more accessible.

🔴 What it changed: Languages like C and C++ required developers to write verbose code and manage memory manually. Python prioritized developer productivity and clean, readable code.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software). Introduced a highly readable, scriptable layer that democratized software development.
📱 Use Today: The dominant programming language for data science, AI development, and academic learning.
Floor 5
1991

Linux — open source conquers the servers

Linus Torvalds releases the Linux kernel as a free, open-source project, which grows to run the modern cloud and supercomputers.

🔴 What it changed: Operating systems were expensive, proprietary commercial systems. Linux provided a free, highly stable Unix-compatible kernel that anyone could inspect and run.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Created the ultimate open-source infrastructure for operating systems.
📱 Use Today: Powers 96% of the world's top servers, all Android phones, and every single supercomputer on Earth.
Floor 3
1993

Mosaic — the web gets graphics

The NCSA releases Mosaic, the first popular web browser displaying images inline with text, sparking the 1990s dot-com boom.

🔴 What it changed: The web was text-only, requiring images to be opened in separate windows. Mosaic displayed images directly inline with text inside a single window.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 4 (Networking). Transformed the web from an academic research index into a visual media publisher.
📱 Use Today: The architectural ancestor of Chrome, Safari, and Firefox, establishing the visual layout of the modern web.
Floor 7
1994

SSL & E-Commerce — securing the web

Netscape introduces SSL, encrypting web traffic and enabling the first secure online purchases, launching the digital commerce era.

🔴 What it changed: Web traffic was sent in plain text, making it trivial for hackers to steal credit card numbers. SSL encrypted the connection, establishing trust.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 1 (Theory). Applied public-key cryptography to web traffic, allowing commerce to exist on the internet.
📱 Use Today: Enables all secure online transactions, from shopping on Amazon to logging into your online banking.
Floor 4
1995

Java & JavaScript — interactive web pages

Sun launches Java for cross-platform server code, and Netscape introduces JavaScript to run code directly inside browsers.

🔴 What it changed: Web pages were static, read-only documents. JavaScript allowed browsers to execute code locally in real-time, making pages interactive.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 5 (Software). Transformed the web browser from a document viewer into an application platform.
📱 Use Today: Powers all modern interactive web features, from Google Maps' smooth scrolling to Facebook's real-time feed updates.
Floor 7
1997

Deep Blue defeats Kasparov — brute-force mastery

IBM's Deep Blue supercomputer defeats chess champion Garry Kasparov, searching up to 200 million board positions per second.

🔴 What it changed: Chess was considered a benchmark of human strategic genius. Deep Blue proved that brute-force calculations combined with hand-coded heuristics could defeat the world's best player.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Proved that parallel computer processors could solve complex strategic search problems.
📱 Use Today: Demonstrated the power of dedicated, parallel compute engines for solving complex, high-dimensional problems.
Floor 6
1998

PageRank — mapping web relevance

Larry Page and Sergey Brin invent PageRank, sorting search results by counting how many other pages link to them.

🔴 What it changed: Search engines indexed pages by counting how many times a keyword appeared on a page, leading to spam and irrelevant results. PageRank measured the web's link graph.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 1 (Theory). Applied linear algebra (eigenvectors) to rank the relevance of the web.
📱 Use Today: Launced Google, defining how humanity indexes and retrieves information on the web.
Floor 5
1999

GeForce 256 — birth of the GPU

NVIDIA releases the GeForce 256, defining the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) by shifting 3D math calculations from the CPU to a dedicated chip.

🔴 What it changed: Computer CPUs had to handle both general software logic and intensive 3D graphics math, leading to performance bottlenecks.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Birthed a dedicated silicon chip designed for parallel matrix mathematics.
📱 Use Today: The architectural ancestor of the hardware running all modern AI training and high-performance computing.
Floor 2
2004

MapReduce — parallel big data processing

Google designs MapReduce, allowing developers to process massive datasets in parallel across thousands of cheap computers.

🔴 What it changed: Big data required expensive, massive supercomputers. MapReduce split files into chunks and processed them in parallel on clusters of cheap consumer servers.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 4 (Networking). Shifted data processing from single mainframes to distributed networks of servers.
📱 Use Today: Laid the foundation for modern big data systems, cloud computing scale, and data pipelines.
Floor 5
2005

Git — decentralized software collaboration

Linus Torvalds designs Git, allowing decentralized version control and massive software collaboration.

🔴 What it changed: Version control was centralized, slow, and prone to conflicts. Git allowed every developer to have a local copy of the code history, merging changes seamlessly.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 7 (Humans/Apps). Redefined how software engineering teams collaborate globally.
📱 Use Today: The industry standard for version control, powering GitHub and the collaboration of millions of software developers daily.
Floor 5
Era 4

The Mobile & Cloud Era (2006–2016)

Renting elastic scale in the cloud, pocket-sized touch screens, and the neural network renaissance.

2006

AWS — computing becomes a utility

Amazon launches EC2 and S3, letting companies rent computing power and storage on-demand over the internet.

🔴 What it changed: Startups had to spend thousands of dollars buying and maintaining physical server hardware in data centers before launching a website. AWS turned servers into utility bills.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Virtualized server hardware, abstracting the physical machine layer into the cloud.
📱 Use Today: Powers the modern internet infrastructure, hosting startups and enterprises alike without requiring local physical servers.
Floor 4
2007

The iPhone — pocket-sized mobile computing

Apple launches the iPhone, combining multi-touch screens, mobile internet, and a desktop-grade browser into a pocket computer.

🔴 What it changed: Smartphones were clunky business tools with physical keys and crippled web browsers. The iPhone made software visually interactive via touch.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Pushed screen, battery, and low-power chip hardware to integrate into pocket sizes.
📱 Use Today: Birthed the modern mobile app economy, shifting software usage from office desktops to people's pockets.
Floor 7
2007

CUDA — the GPU becomes a math accelerator

NVIDIA launches CUDA, a programming model letting developers write general-purpose math calculations on GPUs.

🔴 What it changed: Graphics cards were fast at matrix math but could only be programmed using graphics code. CUDA opened GPUs for general scientific calculations.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Created a software translation layer that turned graphics hardware into general math accelerators.
📱 Use Today: The software backbone for all modern AI training, scientific simulations, and deep learning.
Floor 3
2008

Android & the App Store — the mobile ecosystem

Apple launches the App Store, and Google releases Android, creating the mobile app distribution ecosystem.

🔴 What it changed: Mobile software was controlled by carrier telcos and required custom, clunky installations. App stores consolidated distribution into simple marketplaces.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 5 (Software). Transformed software delivery from desktop web installs to direct, mobile-first updates.
📱 Use Today: Powers the global mobile app economy, defining how software is distributed to billions of people.
Floor 7
2008

Bitcoin & Blockchain — decentralized trustless ledger

Satoshi Nakamoto publishes the Bitcoin whitepaper, combining peer-to-peer networking and cryptography to create a decentralized database.

🔴 What it changed: Digital transactions required a trusted third-party mediator (like a bank) to prevent double-spending. Bitcoin used cryptographic proof-of-work consensus.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 1 (Theory) & Floor 4 (Networking). Resolved the double-spending database trust problem without a central authority.
📱 Use Today: Launched the cryptocurrency industry and decentralized ledger technology (Web3).
Floor 5
2009

ImageNet — scaling AI training data

Fei-Fei Li and team release ImageNet, a massive database of over 14 million hand-labeled images to train vision models.

🔴 What it changed: AI research focused on tweaking neural network algorithms on tiny, clean datasets. ImageNet proved that scaling raw training data was the key to unlocking AI performance.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Data & Software) → Floor 6 (AI). Shifted the AI paradigm from algorithmic complexity to data scaling.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark dataset that catalyzed the modern deep learning revolution.
Floor 5
2010

Rust Language — compile-time memory safety

Mozilla announces Rust, introducing compile-time memory safety checked strictly without a garbage collector.

🔴 What it changed: Systems programming was forced to choose between the speed of C/C++ (which was highly vulnerable to memory bugs) and the safety of managed languages (which were slow).
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Data & Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Guaranteed memory safety at the compiler level without runtime performance penalties.
📱 Use Today: The standard language for writing secure systems code, operating system components (like Linux and Android), and web infrastructure.
Floor 5
2012

AlexNet — the rebirth of deep learning

Alex Krizhevsky and Geoffrey Hinton train a deep neural network on GPUs to win the ImageNet competition by a massive margin.

🔴 What it changed: Computer vision relied on hand-coded feature algorithms designed by humans. AlexNet proved that neural networks could learn features automatically from data.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 2 (Hardware) & Floor 5 (Data). Reunited ImageNet data and GPU parallel hardware to launch the modern AI era.
📱 Use Today: The event that convinced the tech industry that deep learning was the future of artificial intelligence.
Floor 6
2013

Docker — OS-level containerization

Solomon Hykes and team release Docker, popularizing containerization by packaging applications with all settings into portable containers.

🔴 What it changed: Developers suffered from 'it works on my machine'—software failed in production due to minor differences in operating system environments.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Virtualized the operating system layer, allowing software to run identically anywhere.
📱 Use Today: The industry standard for packaging and deploying modern cloud-based web applications.
Floor 3
2014

GANs — competitive deep learning

Ian Goodfellow introduces Generative Adversarial Networks, setting two neural networks to compete to generate realistic data.

🔴 What it changed: Deep learning was great at classifying data (like recognizing cats) but struggled to generate new, high-fidelity content.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI). Created a self-improving training loop that unlocked generative synthetic media.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered generative media, leading to advanced synthetic image, video, and audio generation tools.
Floor 6
2015

Kubernetes — the cloud operating system

Google open-sources Kubernetes, an orchestration system to automate the scaling and management of containerized applications.

🔴 What it changed: Managing thousands of isolated containers (Docker) manually across different physical servers was impossible for engineering teams.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 4 (Networking). Automated cloud hardware scheduling and container load-balancing.
📱 Use Today: The standard 'cloud operating system' that manages the infrastructure of virtually every major web scale company.
Floor 3
2016

AlphaGo — mastering intuitive strategy

DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats Go champion Lee Sedol, using deep neural networks and reinforcement learning to evaluate board positions.

🔴 What it changed: Go was considered impossible for computers because it had too many board configurations for brute-force calculations. AlphaGo learned by playing millions of games against itself.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Demonstrated that deep reinforcement learning could master intuitive, high-dimensional tasks.
📱 Use Today: The landmark proof that AI could discover creative, non-human strategies in complex environments.

2017

★ The Transformer — parallelizing language

Google researchers publish 'Attention Is All You Need', introducing the Transformer architecture to process text sequences in parallel.

🔴 What it changed: Language models read text sequentially word-by-word, which was too slow to train on GPUs and lost context over long paragraphs.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI) → All Floors. By converting language into parallel matrix math, it unlocked the ability to scale AI to the entire internet.
📱 Use Today: The fundamental neural network architecture powering ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and all modern LLMs.
Floor 1 → all floors
2018–20

Scaling Up — the rise of large language models

AI labs scale up the Transformer architecture, showing that increasing size leads to emergent logical capabilities.

🔴 What it changed: AI tools were built and fine-tuned for narrow, specific tasks (like translation). Scaling proved that massive models trained on text could solve generalized reasoning tasks.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Triggered a massive rush to build warehouse-sized AI hardware clusters.
📱 Use Today: Established the scaling laws that guide the development of all frontier AI research labs.
Floor 6
2021

GitHub Copilot — AI pair programming

GitHub and OpenAI launch Copilot, the first widely adopted AI coding assistant integrated inside developer text editors.

🔴 What it changed: Developers had to write all code manually, searching documentation websites for syntax examples and APIs. Copilot integrated LLMs into the workspace.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 5 (Software). Shifted software development from manual syntax writing to visual AI prompting.
📱 Use Today: The daily coding assistant for millions of software engineers, boosting developer productivity.
Floor 7
2022

ChatGPT — conversational interfaces for AI

OpenAI wraps a conversational chat interface around GPT-3.5, launching ChatGPT and democratizing access to LLMs.

🔴 What it changed: Language models required complex, technical prompting via APIs. ChatGPT wrapped them in a simple chat box, making AI accessible to the general public.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 6 (AI). Shifted AI usage from developers to the mass consumer market.
📱 Use Today: The fastest-adopted consumer app in history, initiating the global generative AI boom.
Floor 7
2023

GPT-4 — multimodal scale

OpenAI releases GPT-4, a large multimodal model accepting both image and text inputs with human-level performance.

🔴 What it changed: Language models were text-only and couldn't perceive the visual world. GPT-4 unified text and vision, allowing the model to reason about charts and diagrams.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI). Unified vision and language processing into a single neural network architecture.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark engine for advanced coding, analysis, and visual reasoning tasks.
Floor 6
2024

Agentic AI & MCP — software gets its hands

Anthropic open-sources the Model Context Protocol (MCP), creating a standard for AI models to connect to local files and APIs.

🔴 What it changed: AI models were isolated in chat sandboxes, unable to take actions. MCP created a standard connection for models to interact with local files and run tools.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 5 (Software) & Floor 7 (Apps). Shifted AI from a passive text chatbot to an active, autonomous software agent.
📱 Use Today: Allows coding assistants like this one to run commands, edit files, and build applications autonomously on your machine.
Floor 6
2025

DGX Blackwell — local supercomputing

NVIDIA ships Blackwell-based supercomputing clusters, bringing data-center-grade neural network processing directly to local scale.

🔴 What it changed: Renting AI clusters in the cloud was highly expensive and raised privacy concerns. Blackwell packed data-center-grade parallel power into desktop supercomputers.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 6 (AI). Brought local silicon hardware to match the scaling demands of modern models.
📱 Use Today: Enables companies to run and train massive generative AI models locally and securely.

2025–26

Reasoning Models — inference-time compute scaling

AI labs introduce reasoning models (like OpenAI o1), which spend time 'thinking' before responding, scaling compute at inference time.

🔴 What it changed: Models generated text instantly tokens-by-tokens without self-correction. Reasoning models run an internal reinforcement learning search loop before outputting text.
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Applied mathematical search trees (system-2 thinking) to refine text generation.
📱 Use Today: Enables models to solve highly complex, multi-step coding, mathematics, and logic problems that standard LLMs fail at.
