Tower/Timeline
Nothing was invented in a vacuum

The timeline, cause by cause

Over 180 years of computing history, oldest first. Each milestone is coloured by the floor of the tower it belongs to — and for almost every one, there is a real need that forced it into existence. The army needed firing tables; the phone company needed a reliable switch; the air force and NASA needed computers small enough to fly. Read the "why" lines: that is where the story lives.

Era 1

The Pioneer Era (1843–1956)

From the first mechanical algorithms to the math of universal computers and initial learning machines.

1843

Lovelace's Algorithm — the first computer program

Ada Lovelace writes the first algorithm designed to be carried out by a machine, recognizing that computers could manipulate symbols beyond numbers, including music and art.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Before Lovelace, mechanical calculators were seen strictly as number crunchers. She proved that machines could process any symbolic data.
⚡ Bottleneck: Calculations were bound to physical mechanical gears and static arithmetic operations.
🏭 Catalyst: Babbage's proposed Analytical Engine, which Lovelace translated and extensively annotated.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 5 (Data & Software). Birthed the concept of software 100 years before physical computers could run it.
🔮 Next Domino: Inspired Turing's paper on universal computing machines that run code (1936).
📱 Use Today: It is why your phone doesn't just do math, but displays photos, plays music, and streams video.
Floor 1
1854

Boolean Algebra — the logic of 1s and 0s

George Boole publishes a mathematical system of logic where variables can only have two values: True (1) or False (0), combined using operations like AND, OR, and NOT.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Logical reasoning was a branch of philosophy. Boole turned it into exact algebra—proving logic could be calculated like math.
⚡ Bottleneck: Logical deduction was verbal and philosophical, making it impossible to calculate or mechanize.
🏭 Catalyst: Boole sought to express the laws of human thought and logical reasoning in a formal algebraic language.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Provided the mathematical framework that Shannon later used to design electrical logic gates.
🔮 Next Domino: Enabled Claude Shannon to prove that electrical switches could calculate logic (1937).
📱 Use Today: Every database query, search filter, and chip transaction runs on Boolean logic.
Floor 1
1879

Frege's Formal Logic

Gottlob Frege publishes Begriffsschrift, introducing first-order predicate logic with quantifiers and formal proofs.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Logic was limited to simple categorical statements. Frege introduced variables, relations, and quantifiers, creating a language that could express any mathematical reasoning.
⚡ Bottleneck: Boolean algebra could not represent complex mathematical proofs or statements with multiple variables and relations.
🏭 Catalyst: Frege wanted to prove that all mathematics could be reduced to formal logic (logicism).
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 5 (Data & Software). Provided the mathematical foundation for formal programming languages, compilers, and database query languages.
🔮 Next Domino: Inspired Kurt Gödel to formalize mathematical logic systems, leading to his Incompleteness Theorems (1931).
📱 Use Today: First-order logic is the foundation of type theory in programming languages, formal software verification, and SQL database query engines.
Floor 1
1890

The Hollerith Tabulator — the birth of automated data

Herman Hollerith designs an electromechanical tabulating machine that uses punched paper cards to record and count census data.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Counting data was done entirely by hand. Hollerith used electrical wires running through card holes to automate counting, reducing tabulating time from years to months.
⚡ Bottleneck: Human counting limits: sorting census data by hand was so slow the results would be outdated before they were finished.
🏭 Catalyst: The US Census Bureau needed to compile massive amounts of demographic data for the 1890 census.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 5 (Data). Birthed electromechanical hardware that later evolved into digital storage.
🔮 Next Domino: Led to punched card systems that stored the first digital computer databases.
📱 Use Today: Hollerith's company merged to become IBM, founding the enterprise data processing industry.
Floor 2
1906

The Vacuum Tube

Lee de Forest invents the triode vacuum tube (Audion), the first electronic component that can amplify electrical signals and act as a high-speed switch.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Computing switches were slow, physical, moving electromechanical relays. Vacuum tubes used controlled flows of electrons in a vacuum, switching thousands of times faster with no moving parts.
⚡ Bottleneck: Mechanical gears and electromagnetic relays had physical speed limits and wore out quickly under heavy usage.
🏭 Catalyst: The need to amplify weak electrical audio signals in early wireless telegraphy and telephone lines.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Enabled the transition from slow electromechanical tabulators to high-speed electronic digital computers.
🔮 Next Domino: Provided the high-speed electronic switching elements used to build Colossus (1943) and ENIAC (1945).
📱 Use Today: Though replaced by silicon transistors, vacuum tubes pioneered the concept of purely electronic, solid-state logic switching.
Floor 2
1931

Gödel's Incompleteness

Kurt Gödel proves that any consistent formal mathematical system contains true statements that cannot be proven within the system.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Mathematicians believed that every mathematical statement could either be proven or disproven using formal rules. Gödel proved that mathematics is fundamentally incomplete.
⚡ Bottleneck: The belief that mathematical logic could be perfectly completed, which led to a search for a mechanical proof method for all math.
🏭 Catalyst: David Hilbert's challenge to mathematicians to prove that mathematics is complete, consistent, and decidable.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory). Shattered the dream of a universal mechanical proof system, forcing logicians to define what computation actually means.
🔮 Next Domino: Prompted Alan Turing to design his abstract paper machine to prove that certain mathematical problems are undecidable (1936).
📱 Use Today: Defines the absolute limits of static analysis compilers, automated theorem provers, and software verification tools.
Floor 1
1936

Turing's Paper Machine — the math that begot code

Alan Turing designs an abstract mathematical machine that reads and writes symbols on tape, proving a single machine could perform any possible calculation.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: People believed each task required a custom calculator (one machine for division, one for sorting). Turing proved a single machine could run any possible program.
⚡ Bottleneck: Calculators were single-purpose; changing a task meant building a brand new physical machine.
🏭 Catalyst: Mathematicians trying to prove whether there were mathematical problems that could never be solved.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 3 (Architecture) & Floor 5 (Software). Defined the general-purpose computer and separated software from hardware.
🔮 Next Domino: Provided the theoretical blueprint for von Neumann to design stored-program CPUs (1945).
📱 Use Today: Why your smartphone can be a camera, GPS, or a game console simply by loading different apps.
Floor 1
1936

Lambda Calculus — the other half of computation

Alonzo Church invents a tiny symbolic language where everything — numbers, logic, even whole programs — is built from nothing but functions that take a function and return a function.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: In the very same year as Turing's machine, Church found a second, completely equivalent way to define "computable" — pure functions instead of a tape. Two roads, one destination.
⚡ Bottleneck: Mathematicians had no precise definition of what it means for something to be "calculable by a definite procedure".
🏭 Catalyst: Hilbert's decision problem — the same open challenge that drove Gödel and Turing — demanded a formal model of computation.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 5 (Software). Became the mathematical skeleton of every functional programming language.
🔮 Next Domino: Directly inspired John McCarthy's LISP (1958), the first language to treat functions as data.
📱 Use Today: Every lambda, every => arrow function, every map/filter in Python, JavaScript, and even Excel traces straight back to Church.
Floor 1
1937

Shannon's Logic Gates — bridging math and switches

Claude Shannon proves that electric relays and switches can physically implement Boolean logical operations, establishing digital circuit design.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Switching networks for telephone routing were designed by trial-and-error. Shannon proved that electrical switches could calculate Boolean logic.
⚡ Bottleneck: Telephone routing circuits were becoming too complex to design manually without mathematical rules.
🏭 Catalyst: Shannon working on telephone switching networks at Bell Labs while studying Boolean logic at MIT.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Translated abstract logic into physical, silicon-based computer hardware.
🔮 Next Domino: Formed the hardware design language used to construct electronic computers like ENIAC (1945).
📱 Use Today: The basic architecture of every silicon processor in every computer, router, and smartphone on Earth.
Floor 2
1943

McCulloch-Pitts Neuron — computing with biological logic

Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts publish a mathematical model of a simplified biological neuron, showing it can calculate any logical function.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Biology and logic circuits were studied separately. They proved that simplified networks of binary neurons could compute any mathematical function.
⚡ Bottleneck: There was no scientific connection showing how physical brains or electrical circuits could produce logic and thought.
🏭 Catalyst: A desire to model how biological networks in the brain represent concepts and make decisions.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI). The mathematical birth of artificial neural networks, modeled on biological brains.
🔮 Next Domino: Laid the foundation for Frank Rosenblatt to build the learning Perceptron machine (1957).
📱 Use Today: The absolute ancestral math behind modern neural networks, deep learning, and AI.
Floor 6
1944

Colossus — codebreaking in wartime

Tommy Flowers designs Colossus, using vacuum tubes to decipher encrypted telegraph messages at electronic speeds.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Codebreaking was done by hand or mechanical machines. Colossus used vacuum tubes to calculate logical combinations at electronic speeds.
⚡ Bottleneck: Mechanical gears and switches were limited by physical speed limits, which were too slow for wartime decryption.
🏭 Catalyst: British codebreakers at Bletchley Park needed to crack the German Lorenz cipher to understand troop movements.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Proved that vacuum tubes could build reliable, high-speed electronic logic machines.
🔮 Next Domino: Showed that large arrays of vacuum tubes were reliable, inspiring general-purpose projects like ENIAC.
📱 Use Today: Birthed the electronic digital computer age, proving that physical hardware could replace mechanical switches.
Floor 2
1945

ENIAC — electricity replaces human calculators

The world's first general-purpose electronic computer is built: a 30-ton giant containing 17,468 glowing vacuum tubes, computing 5,000 additions per second.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Ballistic calculations took weeks for human 'computers' using mechanical calculators. ENIAC calculated them in seconds using purely electronic vacuum tubes.
⚡ Bottleneck: Calculating artillery trajectories manually was too slow to support active military artillery crews in WWII.
🏭 Catalyst: The US Army needed firing tables for artillery crews and funded a project for an electronic calculator.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). The world's first general-purpose electronic digital computer, proving the feasibility of large-scale computing.
🔮 Next Domino: Prompted von Neumann to address the bottleneck of rewiring ENIAC by designing stored-program memory.
📱 Use Today: The starting point of all electronic computing hardware in human history.
Floor 2
1945

The Stored-Program Concept — putting code in the pantry

John von Neumann proposes storing program instructions in the same memory as data, allowing the machine to be reprogrammed in seconds.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Reprogramming a computer meant spending days physically rewiring cables and switches. Storing instructions as data made computers instantly reprogrammable.
⚡ Bottleneck: Computers were hard-wired; changing the software required shutting down the machine and physically replugging wires for days.
🏭 Catalyst: Von Neumann observing the bottleneck of reprograming the ENIAC while designing the EDVAC computer.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Turned computers from hardwired calculators into dynamically programmable processors.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered the CPU architecture that allowed high-level compilers (Grace Hopper, 1952) to compile programs into memory.
📱 Use Today: Why you can switch apps instantly or download new software without rebuilding your phone.
Floor 3
1947

The Transistor — the solid-state revolution

Bell Labs physicists create the first transistor, a solid-state switch made of silicon that replaces hot, fragile vacuum tubes.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Hot, fragile vacuum tubes broke constantly and used massive power. Transistors used solid-state silicon switches that never burned out and used microscopic power.
⚡ Bottleneck: Vacuum tubes were bulky, consumed massive power, and burned out frequently, limiting the size and scale of computers.
🏭 Catalyst: Bell Labs needed a highly reliable, solid-state electronic switch to route millions of long-distance telephone calls.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Allowed computers to shrink from the size of a room to the size of a chip.
🔮 Next Domino: Provided the fundamental building block that Noyce and Kilby placed on the first integrated circuit (1958).
📱 Use Today: Billions of transistors fit on a single fingernail-sized processor, powering every modern device.
Floor 2
1948

Information Theory — measuring the invisible "bit"

Claude Shannon proves that all human communication can be boiled down to binary 'bits' (1s and 0s) and transmitted perfectly over noisy wires.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Communications engineers designed radios and phone lines by trial-and-error. Shannon proved all media could be digitized into 'bits' and mathematically defined the speed limit of data transmission.
⚡ Bottleneck: Noisy transmission lines corrupted data, and there was no mathematical way to measure a channel's capacity.
🏭 Catalyst: Bell Labs wanting to maximize the capacity and efficiency of transmitting signals through telephone lines.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 4 (Networking). Laid the mathematical foundation for digitizing all human data.
🔮 Next Domino: Provided the math for data compression and error correction, later enabling dial-up modems and packet-switched routing.
📱 Use Today: Powers every video stream, MP3, 5G signal, and optical fiber connection on Earth.
Floor 1
1950

The Turing Test — defining machine intelligence

Alan Turing proposes the 'Imitation Game' as a practical benchmark to answer the question, 'Can machines think?'

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Philosophers argued endlessly over whether a machine could have a 'soul'. Turing replaced the debate with a practical conversational test.
⚡ Bottleneck: There was no consensus on how to evaluate or define 'thinking' or 'intelligence' in an artificial machine.
🏭 Catalyst: The creation of early electronic computers, which prompted philosophers to debate machine consciousness.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI) & Floor 7 (Humans). Established conversation as the benchmark of artificial intelligence.
🔮 Next Domino: Prompted researchers at the Dartmouth Workshop (1956) to formally establish AI as a field of study.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark we still use to evaluate models like ChatGPT and Claude.
Floor 6
1952

The First Compiler — teaching computers English

Grace Hopper designs a system that lets humans write code in English-like words, which the program automatically translates into binary.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Programming required writing endless lists of raw binary codes. Grace Hopper designed a program that translated English-like commands into machine code automatically.
⚡ Bottleneck: Programming in raw machine language (octal or binary) was too slow, making software development a massive bottleneck.
🏭 Catalyst: Hopper's frustration with writing repetitive machine math codes while working on the UNIVAC computer.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Birthed the concept of high-level programming languages.
🔮 Next Domino: Led directly to high-level commercial programming languages like FORTRAN (1957) and COBOL (1959).
📱 Use Today: Every line of code written by modern software developers is compiled using Grace Hopper's core concept.
Floor 5
1953

Magnetic-Core Memory — the first true RAM

Jay Forrester's team threads a grid of tiny magnetic rings on wires, each ring storing one bit by its magnetisation — fast, reliable memory a computer can read and rewrite in millionths of a second.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Early machines remembered data with delay-line tubes and spinning drums that were slow and fragile. Core memory held bits in stable magnetic rings with no moving parts — instantly accessible at any address.
⚡ Bottleneck: The stored-program idea needed somewhere fast to keep code and data, but existing memory was too slow and unreliable to deliver on the promise.
🏭 Catalyst: MIT's Whirlwind, a real-time flight simulator and air-defence computer, needed memory quick enough to keep up with live radar data.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Gave the stored-program computer the fast working memory it always assumed but never had.
🔮 Next Domino: Became the standard main memory for two decades — including the mainframes of IBM System/360 (1964).
📱 Use Today: We still say a program "dumps core" when it crashes — and the word "RAM" means exactly what core memory pioneered: random, instant access to any byte.
Floor 2
1956

Dartmouth Workshop — naming "Artificial Intelligence"

A summer gathering of researchers coins the term 'Artificial Intelligence', establishing it as a new scientific field.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Separated researchers worked on 'cybernetics' and 'automata'. The workshop united them under the name 'Artificial Intelligence'.
⚡ Bottleneck: Research into thinking machines was fragmented across different academic disciplines without a unified direction.
🏭 Catalyst: A proposal by John McCarthy to assemble researchers for two months to brainstorm how to make machines simulate human learning and intelligence.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI). Established AI as a distinct academic and engineering discipline.
🔮 Next Domino: Spurred funding that led to early AI breakthroughs like the Perceptron (1957) and symbolic logic systems.
📱 Use Today: Launched the field of research that created all modern AI tools.
Floor 6
Era 2

The Microchip & Silicon Era (1957–1974)

Shrinking physical gates onto pieces of rock, leading to OS, database, and network standards.

1957

The Mark I Perceptron — the first learning neural network

Frank Rosenblatt builds the Perceptron, the first neural network machine that learns weights and associations from data.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Early AI researchers programmed computers with strict, hand-written logical rules. The Perceptron learned dynamically from data by adjusting its circuits automatically using a mathematical error-correction rule when it makes mistakes.
⚡ Bottleneck: Hand-coding every logical rule was too slow and could not adapt to noisy real-world data like images.
🏭 Catalyst: Rosenblatt wanted to build a machine that could mimic the visual learning capabilities of biological brains.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Birthed the field of neural networks that learn from data rather than rules.
🔮 Next Domino: Laid the groundwork for deep learning, though it stalled until backpropagation (1986) solved its multi-layer bottleneck.
📱 Use Today: The conceptual ancestor of all neural network training algorithms.
Floor 6
1957

FORTRAN — software for scientists

IBM releases FORTRAN (Formula Translation), letting engineers write mathematical equations like A = B + C instead of raw machine numbers.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Programming required writing raw numbers for CPU instructions. FORTRAN let programmers write math-like formulas, boosting developer productivity ten-fold.
⚡ Bottleneck: Writing software was so tedious that the cost of programming exceeded the cost of running the computer itself.
🏭 Catalyst: IBM wanting to sell computers to scientific markets, which required a language that scientists could easily write.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Established compiler technology that maps high-level human logic to physical CPU instructions.
🔮 Next Domino: Proved that compilers could generate code that was just as fast as hand-written machine code, establishing high-level programming.
📱 Use Today: Modern scientific and engineering software still compiles using derivatives of FORTRAN's optimized math compilers.
Floor 5
1958

LISP — the language of symbolic reasoning

John McCarthy designs LISP, introducing functional programming, automatic garbage collection, and recursive function logic.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Early programming languages like FORTRAN were built strictly for numerical calculations. LISP processed symbols, concepts, and lists of words to model logical thinking.
⚡ Bottleneck: Early languages had no way to manipulate symbolic lists or dynamic concepts required for logical reasoning.
🏭 Catalyst: AI researchers needed a language to represent relationships, rules, and mathematical proofs for early logic solvers.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 6 (AI). The language that defined symbolic AI reasoning for decades.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered automated memory management (garbage collection) used in most modern high-level languages.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered features like garbage collection and dynamic typing used in Python, JavaScript, and Java.
Floor 5
1958–59

The Integrated Circuit — carving circuits in stone

Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce independently find a way to place multiple transistors, resistors, and wires onto a single slice of silicon.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Building a computer required soldering thousands of tiny parts by hand. A single bad solder joint ruined the machine. Carving components onto one silicon block solved this 'tyranny of numbers'.
⚡ Bottleneck: The 'tyranny of numbers': physical cabling and hand-soldering of individual transistors capped how small or reliable a computer could be.
🏭 Catalyst: Texas Instruments and Fairchild Semiconductor competing to build micro-electronics for industrial and military needs.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Transformed electronic manufacturing from custom assembly to mass-produced printing.
🔮 Next Domino: Allowed NASA and the military to buy chips in bulk, making silicon chips cheap enough to build microprocessors (1971).
📱 Use Today: The reason every microchip in your phone and computer can contain billions of transistors in a tiny space.
Floor 2
1959

COBOL — standardizing business software

A joint committee defines COBOL, letting programmers write business software in simple, English-like syntax.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Software was custom-tailored for scientific math and ran on only one computer model. COBOL created a portable, business-oriented language for billing and payroll.
⚡ Bottleneck: Businesses could not easily hire mathematical programmers to write payroll, billing, and accounting systems.
🏭 Catalyst: The US Department of Defense and computer manufacturers collaborating to standardize a business programming language.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 7 (Humans/Apps). Allowed companies to run software across different hardware manufacturers.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered portable, platform-independent enterprise applications, proving software could outlive hardware.
📱 Use Today: Still runs behind the scenes in many banking, credit card, and government systems worldwide.
Floor 5
1962–69

Minuteman & Apollo — funding the chip revolution

The US military and NASA buy integrated circuits in massive quantities, single-handedly funding the scale-up of chip factories.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Early silicon chips were custom-built, unreliable, and cost thousands of dollars. NASA and the military bought them in bulk for guidance computers, driving down costs and forcing production standards.
⚡ Bottleneck: Early silicon chips were too expensive ($1000+ each) and had low manufacturing yields, making commercial use impossible.
🏭 Catalyst: The Cold War arms race and the Space Race: missiles and Apollo capsules needed extremely lightweight computers to fly.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 7 (Apps). Funded the scale-up of silicon factories, making microchips cheap enough for consumer products.
🔮 Next Domino: Drove the price of silicon chips down by 99%, allowing companies like Intel to design personal microprocessors.
📱 Use Today: Without this government scale-up, microchips would have remained too expensive for personal computers and smartphones.
Floor 2
1964

IBM System/360 — software standardization

IBM launches a family of computers that all share the same instruction set, allowing software to run unmodified across different sizes of mainframes.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Every computer model had unique hardware. Upgrading machines meant throwing away all code and rewriting it from scratch. IBM standardizes hardware interfaces.
⚡ Bottleneck: Companies upgrading to larger computers had to rewrite all their programs from scratch, costing millions in redundant software engineering.
🏭 Catalyst: IBM risked losing its business market by consolidating its fragmented product lines into a single compatible family.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Decoupled the software layer from physical machine scale.
🔮 Next Domino: Birthed the standard operating system layer, allowing companies to invest in software that would outlast the physical hardware.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered the concept of backwards compatibility that allows old programs to run on modern computers.
Floor 3
1965

Moore's Law — predicting exponential chip scaling

Gordon Moore predicts that the number of transistors on a single silicon microchip will double roughly every two years.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: The silicon industry was in its infancy and seen as risky. Moore's prediction set an exponential development roadmap that guided corporate investments.
⚡ Bottleneck: Without a developmental roadmap, chip companies lacked target goals to plan production technology and investments.
🏭 Catalyst: Electronics Magazine asking Gordon Moore to write an article predicting the future of silicon chips for the next ten years.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware). Became a self-fulfilling prophecy that drove the rapid scaling of hardware performance.
🔮 Next Domino: Forced competing factories to hit doubling targets, leading directly to the microprocessors of the 1970s.
📱 Use Today: The reason computers have consistently become faster, cheaper, and smaller every single year for decades.
Floor 2
1967

Simula — the birth of Object-Oriented Programming

Ole-Johan Dahl and Kristen Nygaard release Simula, introducing the concepts of classes, objects, and inheritance to model physical systems.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Programs were written as sequential lists of instructions. Simula grouped data and actions into modular 'objects' that behave like real-world entities.
⚡ Bottleneck: Writing simulation software (like traffic routing or shipyard schedules) in linear code was too complex and hard to maintain.
🏭 Catalyst: The creators needed to run complex simulations of physical systems like nuclear reactors and shipbuilding operations.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Established Object-Oriented Programming (OOP), structuring how software architectures are designed.
🔮 Next Domino: Directly inspired the creation of Smalltalk, C++, Java, and modern Object-Oriented software architectures.
📱 Use Today: The paradigm that organizes modern programming languages like Java, C++, Python, and C#.
Floor 5
1968

The Mother of All Demos — glimpsing the future

Douglas Engelbart demonstrates the computer mouse, graphical windows, clickable hypertext, real-time text editing, and video conferencing in a single 90-minute demo.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Computers were cold, massive calculators fed on punch cards. Engelbart demonstrated that they could be collaborative, visual tools to extend human intellect.
⚡ Bottleneck: Computers were inaccessible to non-experts, operated solely by feeding stacks of paper punch cards to technicians.
🏭 Catalyst: Engelbart's research group wanting to demonstrate how computers could help people solve complex human problems collaboratively.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Outlined the visual interface concepts that computers spent the next 30 years building.
🔮 Next Domino: Inspired Xerox PARC to build the Alto (1973), which directly shaped the Apple Macintosh (1984).
📱 Use Today: Every element of your desktop interface—mouse, windows, links, and video calls—was shown in this single 1968 demo.
Floor 7
1969

ARPANET — the first node of the internet

Four university computers are linked together, successfully exchanging messages by breaking data into packets that find their own way across a network.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Computer networks relied on dedicated, fragile lines (like phone lines). If one line broke, the connection died. ARPANET introduced packet switching to route data dynamically.
⚡ Bottleneck: Centralized, circuit-switched telephone lines were fragile; a single broken cable could collapse an entire communication line.
🏭 Catalyst: The US military (via DARPA) wanting to build a robust, decentralized computer network that could survive partial cable cuts.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 4 (Networking) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Converted computing from standalone hardware into a connected mesh.
🔮 Next Domino: Laid the packet-switching groundwork that Kahn and Cerf normalized into the global TCP/IP internet protocol (1973).
📱 Use Today: The network design that became the global internet.
Floor 4
1969–71

Unix — lean, modular operating systems

Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie create Unix, an operating system built on a simple philosophy: write small programs that do one thing well and chain them together.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Operating systems were massive, bloated, and over-engineered. Unix proved that a simple, modular system running on cheap minicomputers was more powerful and easier to maintain.
⚡ Bottleneck: Operating systems of the 1960s (like Multics) were massive, slow, and ran only on proprietary, expensive mainframes.
🏭 Catalyst: The creators needed a lightweight operating system to play games and run text formatting tools on an old PDP-7 minicomputer.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Created a clean system interface (the command line and files) for programmers to build tools.
🔮 Next Domino: Inspired Dennis Ritchie to create the C language (1972) to rewrite Unix so it could run on different hardware platforms.
📱 Use Today: The design foundation for macOS, Linux, Android, and the cloud servers running the internet.
Floor 3
1970

The Relational Database — data in clean tables

Edgar Codd proposes organizing database records into simple rows and columns across linked tables, separating the database's logical structure from physical storage.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Database programs had to know exactly which disk sectors the data was written to. If you changed the physical layout, all the code broke. Codd separated logical tables from physical sectors.
⚡ Bottleneck: Early databases stored data in rigid hierarchies; querying them required understanding the exact physical disk locations of data.
🏭 Catalyst: IBM needed to find a way to let business users query databases without requiring them to write complex storage navigation code.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Decoupled data query logic from physical storage hardware.
🔮 Next Domino: Prompted Chamberlin and Boyce to design SQL (1974) as a declarative language to query these tables.
📱 Use Today: Every transactional database, SQL server, and user registry uses Codd's relational model.
Floor 5
1971

Email & the @ Sign — the network's first killer app

Ray Tomlinson sends the first message between two computers on a network, borrowing the lonely @ key to separate the user from the machine — user@host — an address format still used by billions today.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Messages could only be left for other users of the same computer. Tomlinson let a message hop across the network to a person on a different machine entirely.

🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Data & Software) → Floor 6 (AI). Shifted the AI paradigm from algorithmic complexity to data scaling.
🔮 Next Domino: Enabled Hinton and Krizhevsky to train AlexNet (2012) on GPUs, triggering the deep learning revolution.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark dataset that catalyzed the modern deep learning revolution.

2010

Rust Language — compile-time memory safety

Mozilla announces Rust, introducing compile-time memory safety checked strictly without a garbage collector.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Systems programming was forced to choose between the speed of C/C++ (which was highly vulnerable to memory bugs) and the safety of managed languages (which were slow).
⚡ Bottleneck: C/C++ programs ran at raw hardware speeds but suffered from memory bugs (like buffer overflows), causing 70% of security exploits.
🏭 Catalyst: Mozilla needing to write a safe, highly parallel web browser engine (Servo) that wouldn't suffer from constant security crashes.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 5 (Data & Software) → Floor 3 (Architecture). Guaranteed memory safety at the compiler level without runtime performance penalties.
🔮 Next Domino: Adopted by major systems (Linux kernel, Windows, Android) as a safe replacement for writing core system software.
📱 Use Today: The standard language for writing secure systems code, operating system components (like Linux and Android), and web infrastructure.
Floor 5
2012

AlexNet — the rebirth of deep learning

Alex Krizhevsky and Geoffrey Hinton train a deep neural network on GPUs to win the ImageNet competition by a massive margin.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Computer vision relied on hand-coded feature algorithms designed by humans. AlexNet proved that neural networks could learn features automatically from data.
⚡ Bottleneck: Classical computer vision systems were hand-designed by experts and had hit a strict accuracy ceiling on image recognition.
🏭 Catalyst: The availability of the massive ImageNet dataset and NVIDIA CUDA GPUs, allowing deep neural networks to be trained in days instead of months.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 2 (Hardware) & Floor 5 (Data). Reunited ImageNet data and GPU parallel hardware to launch the modern AI era.
🔮 Next Domino: Prompted Google, Facebook, and Baidu to pivot their engineering resources to deep learning, founding modern AI divisions.
📱 Use Today: The event that convinced the tech industry that deep learning was the future of artificial intelligence.
Floor 6
2013

Docker — OS-level containerization

Solomon Hykes and team release Docker, popularizing containerization by packaging applications with all settings into portable containers.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Developers suffered from 'it works on my machine'—software failed in production due to minor differences in operating system environments.
⚡ Bottleneck: Different operating system configurations and library versions caused software to run locally but crash when deployed to servers.
🏭 Catalyst: Dotcloud (a platform-as-a-service company) needing to run and isolate customer programs on shared server operating systems.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 5 (Software). Virtualized the operating system layer, allowing software to run identically anywhere.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered lightweight containerization, forcing Google to design Kubernetes (2015) to manage arrays of containers at scale.
📱 Use Today: The industry standard for packaging and deploying modern cloud-based web applications.

2014

GANs — competitive deep learning

Ian Goodfellow introduces Generative Adversarial Networks, setting two neural networks to compete to generate realistic data.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Deep learning was great at classifying data (like recognizing cats) but struggled to generate new, high-fidelity content.
⚡ Bottleneck: Traditional generative models struggled to produce realistic, sharp images, outputting blurry averages instead.
🏭 Catalyst: Ian Goodfellow brainstorming a way for neural networks to generate sharp, high-fidelity images by competing in a game-theoretic loop.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI). Created a self-improving training loop that unlocked generative synthetic media.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered generative media architectures, leading to deepfake systems and modern image synthesizers.
📱 Use Today: Pioneered generative media, leading to advanced synthetic image, video, and audio generation tools.

2015

Kubernetes — the cloud operating system

Google open-sources Kubernetes, an orchestration system to automate the scaling and management of containerized applications.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Managing thousands of isolated containers (Docker) manually across different physical servers was impossible for engineering teams.
⚡ Bottleneck: Scaling and managing thousands of Docker containers manually across clusters of physical servers was an operational nightmare.
🏭 Catalyst: Google wanting to bring the internal container-scaling capabilities of its 'Borg' system to the public cloud ecosystem.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 3 (Architecture) → Floor 4 (Networking). Automated cloud hardware scheduling and container load-balancing.
🔮 Next Domino: Standardized the 'cloud operating system' layer, enabling scalable, cloud-native enterprise application architectures.
📱 Use Today: The standard 'cloud operating system' that manages the infrastructure of virtually every major web scale company.
Floor 3
2016

AlphaGo — mastering intuitive strategy

DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats Go champion Lee Sedol, using deep neural networks and reinforcement learning to evaluate board positions.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Go was considered impossible for computers because it had too many board configurations for brute-force calculations. AlphaGo learned by playing millions of games against itself.
⚡ Bottleneck: Brute-force board search was useless in Go due to an astronomical number of possible moves, requiring human-like intuition.
🏭 Catalyst: DeepMind wanting to demonstrate that deep neural networks combined with reinforcement learning could master intuitive human tasks.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Demonstrated that deep reinforcement learning could master intuitive, high-dimensional tasks.
🔮 Next Domino: Laid the foundation for AlphaFold to solve the 50-year-old biological mystery of protein folding using neural networks.
📱 Use Today: The landmark proof that AI could discover creative, non-human strategies in complex environments.

The Generative AI Era (2017–2026)

Transformers parallelize sequences, enabling massive language scale and local silicon accelerations.

2017

★ The Transformer — parallelizing language

Google researchers publish 'Attention Is All You Need', introducing the Transformer architecture to process text sequences in parallel.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Language models read text sequentially word-by-word, which was too slow to train on GPUs and lost context over long paragraphs.
⚡ Bottleneck: Sequential text processing: models read word-by-word, meaning training could not be split across parallel GPU processors.
🏭 Catalyst: Google researchers seeking a faster, more accurate translation model that could parse long paragraphs without losing track of early words.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 1 (Theory) → Floor 6 (AI) → All Floors. By converting language into parallel matrix math, it unlocked the ability to scale AI to the entire internet.
🔮 Next Domino: Allowed labs to train massive models on the entire web, leading directly to GPT-3 and ChatGPT (2022).
📱 Use Today: The fundamental neural network architecture powering ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and all modern LLMs.
Floor 1 → all floors
2018–20

Scaling Up — the rise of large language models

AI labs scale up the Transformer architecture, showing that increasing size leads to emergent logical capabilities.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: AI tools were built and fine-tuned for narrow, specific tasks (like translation). Scaling proved that massive models trained on text could solve generalized reasoning tasks.
⚡ Bottleneck: AI research was stuck fine-tuning small models for narrow tasks, believing that generalized reasoning required custom architectures.
🏭 Catalyst: Research showing that scaling model parameters and training data in Transformers led to predictable, exponential intelligence gains.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 2 (Hardware). Triggered a massive rush to build warehouse-sized AI hardware clusters.
🔮 Next Domino: Forced companies to upgrade data center networking (Floor 4) and silicon chips (Floor 2) to support massive compute demands.
📱 Use Today: Established the scaling laws that guide the development of all frontier AI research labs.

2021

GitHub Copilot — AI pair programming

GitHub and OpenAI launch Copilot, the first widely adopted AI coding assistant integrated inside developer text editors.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Developers had to write all code manually, searching documentation websites for syntax examples and APIs. Copilot integrated LLMs into the workspace.
⚡ Bottleneck: Software developers spent hours writing repetitive syntax code and searching documentation for API examples.
🏭 Catalyst: The availability of OpenAI's Codex model, which was trained on public code repositories and integrated into the IDE.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 5 (Software). Shifted software development from manual syntax writing to visual AI prompting.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered LLM-based developer tooling, leading to autonomous coding agents (like this one) and Model Context Protocol standardizations.
📱 Use Today: The daily coding assistant for millions of software engineers, boosting developer productivity.
Floor 7
2022

ChatGPT — conversational interfaces for AI

OpenAI wraps a conversational chat interface around GPT-3.5, launching ChatGPT and democratizing access to LLMs.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Language models required complex, technical prompting via APIs. ChatGPT wrapped them in a simple chat box, making AI accessible to the general public.
⚡ Bottleneck: Language models were highly technical to use, requiring developers to write complex API code and custom prompts.
🏭 Catalyst: OpenAI deciding to wrap an instruction-tuned model in a simple conversational interface to gather public feedback.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 7 (Humans/Apps) → Floor 6 (AI). Shifted AI usage from developers to the mass consumer market.
🔮 Next Domino: Ignited a massive consumer and enterprise rush to adopt LLMs, driving NVIDIA to design Blackwell GPUs.
📱 Use Today: The fastest-adopted consumer app in history, initiating the global generative AI boom.

2023

GPT-4 — multimodal scale

OpenAI releases GPT-4, a large multimodal model accepting both image and text inputs with human-level performance.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Language models were text-only and couldn't perceive the visual world. GPT-4 unified text and vision, allowing the model to reason about charts and diagrams.
⚡ Bottleneck: Text-only limitations: AI models could not perceive or reason about the physical visual world, charts, or diagrams.
🏭 Catalyst: AI labs training models on multimodal datasets (unifying images and text) to allow joint visual-linguistic reasoning.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI). Unified vision and language processing into a single neural network architecture.
🔮 Next Domino: Enabled AI models to reason about visual user interfaces, paving the path for autonomous web browsing and agents.
📱 Use Today: The benchmark engine for advanced coding, analysis, and visual reasoning tasks.

2024

Agentic AI & MCP — software gets its hands

Anthropic open-sources the Model Context Protocol (MCP), creating a standard for AI models to connect to local files and APIs.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: AI models were isolated in chat sandboxes, unable to take actions. MCP created a standard connection for models to interact with local files and run tools.
⚡ Bottleneck: AI systems were isolated chatbots: they could not interact with local files, databases, or APIs without custom, fragile code integrations.
🏭 Catalyst: Anthropic and developer groups realizing that LLMs needed a unified, secure connection standard to act as autonomous agents.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 5 (Software) & Floor 7 (Apps). Shifted AI from a passive text chatbot to an active, autonomous software agent.
🔮 Next Domino: Pioneered autonomous software engineering loops where agents build, compile, and debug applications directly.
📱 Use Today: Allows coding assistants like this one to run commands, edit files, and build applications autonomously on your machine.

2025

DGX Blackwell — local supercomputing

NVIDIA ships Blackwell-based supercomputing clusters, bringing data-center-grade neural network processing directly to local scale.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Renting AI clusters in the cloud was highly expensive and raised privacy concerns. Blackwell packed data-center-grade parallel power into desktop supercomputers.
⚡ Bottleneck: Renting massive GPU clusters in the cloud raised data privacy concerns and incurred extremely high operational costs.
🏭 Catalyst: NVIDIA utilizing its Blackwell chip architecture to pack data-center-grade parallel matrix math power into localized clusters.
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 2 (Hardware) → Floor 6 (AI). Brought local silicon hardware to match the scaling demands of modern models.
🔮 Next Domino: Democratized model pre-training and secure local execution for enterprises, bypassing public cloud latency.
📱 Use Today: Enables companies to run and train massive generative AI models locally and securely.

2025–26

Reasoning Models — inference-time compute scaling

AI labs introduce reasoning models (like OpenAI o1), which spend time 'thinking' before responding, scaling compute at inference time.

The Spark
🔴 Changed: Models generated text instantly tokens-by-tokens without self-correction. Reasoning models run an internal reinforcement learning search loop before outputting text.
⚡ Bottleneck: Standard LLMs generated responses instantly token-by-token, failing at complex math and multi-step logic that required self-correction.
🏭 Catalyst: The limits of pre-training web data, prompting research labs to scale up computing at inference time (reinforcement learning chains).
The Impact
🔗 Floor Impact: Floor 6 (AI) → Floor 1 (Theory). Applied mathematical search trees (system-2 thinking) to refine text generation.
🔮 Next Domino: Allows autonomous agents to perform complex, multi-step debugging and code planning before taking execution actions.
📱 Use Today: Enables models to solve highly complex, multi-step coding, mathematics, and logic problems that standard LLMs fail at.
