Floor 2 · the machine

Physical Hardware

Here the mathematics of Floor 1 becomes a real object you can hold: electricity flowing through switches etched in silicon. This is the story of those switches shrinking — from a room full of glowing tubes in 1945 to billions of transistors on a fingernail, and the chip that now trains AI.

💻 What this floor is

Every computer is, underneath, a vast collection of tiny electrical switches. A switch that is on means 1; off means 0. Arrange enough of them and wire them with the logic from Floor 1, and they can add, compare, remember, and decide. Floor 2 is the physics and engineering of making those switches smaller, faster, cheaper, and more numerous — the single trend that has driven computing for 80 years.

The big ideas

Three layers of real hardware, bottom to top.

🔘 1. The switch: tube → transistor

The first electronic computers used vacuum tubes. Imagine a delicate glass light bulb that acts as a switch. If the bulb glows, it means 1; if dark, it means 0. But because they were glass bulbs, they ran hot, burned out constantly, and attracted real bugs inside (which is why broken code is still called a "bug").

Then in 1947, physicists built the transistor. Think of a transistor as a **tiny electronic water gate**. Apply a tiny drop of electricity to the gate, and it opens a wide canal for a massive flow of electricity to rush through. Because it has no glass, no vacuum, and no moving parts, it never burns out and can be shrunk to near-atomic size.

But simply printing transistors wasn't enough. As chips packed more switches, they drew too much power and got hot enough to melt. In 1963, Frank WanlassFairchild Semiconductor engineer who patented CMOS in 1963. It combines complementary pairs of p-type and n-type transistors so that current only flows when switching states, reducing power use by 99%. patented CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor). By pairing transistors so that one is always off when the other is on, CMOS ensured electricity only flowed during the brief moment a switch flipped. This cut standby power consumption by over 99%, making digital watches, battery-powered laptops, and smartphones possible.

🔎 The scale of the shrink

ENIAC (1945) used roughly 17,000 vacuum tubes and filled a whole room. A modern smartphone chip packs well over ten billion transistors into a space the size of a fingernail. Same idea — an electrical switch — shrunk by a factor of millions.

The real need

The telephone network ran on millions of vacuum tubes that burned out constantly and needed replacing — an unreliable, power-hungry mess.

What it forced

Bell Labs set out to build a switch that never burned out. The result — the transistor — became the building block of all modern electronics.

🔲 2. Putting it all on one chip: the microprocessor

At first, transistors were wired together by hand. The breakthrough was printing thousands of them onto a single piece of silicon. On 15 November 1971, Intel released the Intel 4004The first commercial microprocessor. About 2,300 transistors, a 4-bit design, built on a 10-micrometre process, priced at US$60. — the first commercial microprocessor: an entire computer's "brain" (the CPU) on one chip about the size of a fingernail, holding around 2,300 transistors.

As chip density exploded, designing layouts by drawing wires by hand became impossible. In 1980, Caltech professor Carver MeadCo-author of 'Introduction to VLSI Systems' with Lynn Conway. Pioneered VLSI design rules and coined the term Moore's Law. and Xerox PARC researcher Lynn ConwayCo-author of 'Introduction to VLSI Systems' with Carver Mead. Pioneered structured VLSI design rules and chip multi-project wafers. published standard rules for **VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration)**. By decoupling circuit design from physical physics, they introduced abstract rules that allowed computer scientists to design chips using code-like software tools, turning chip design into a compiled software task.

🔎 Why this changed everything

Once a CPU was one cheap chip, computers could leave the air-conditioned room and go anywhere — onto desks, into cars, into phones. The personal computer, the smartphone, and the smart everything-else all start with the 4004 in 1971.

The real need

A missile or a spacecraft needs a computer that is tiny, light, low-power and rugged. The Minuteman II missileThe Minuteman II guidance computer and the Apollo Guidance Computer were among the first machines built almost entirely from integrated circuits. US space and defence buying was roughly a third of the whole chip market in the 1960s. and the Apollo program needed exactly that.

What it forced

Only the integrated circuit could meet it — so the military and NASA bought enough chips to make them cheap for everyone. The Minuteman missile, more than anything, forced the chip into mass production.

The real need

A Japanese calculator company, Busicom, ordered a set of custom chips for a desktop calculator in 1969.

What it forced

Intel's Ted Hoff proposed one general-purpose programmable chip instead of many special ones — and the microprocessor was born, by accident, out of a calculator order.

🟩 3. Two kinds of brain: CPU and GPU

A CPU (Central Processing Unit) has a few very capable cores. Think of a CPU as a **smart university professor** who can solve complex calculus equations very quickly, but can only solve them one after another (sequentially).

A GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) has thousands of simpler cores. Think of a GPU as a **stadium of 10,000 middle-schoolers** doing simple addition. If you give the professor 10,000 simple additions, it takes them hours to write down the answers one-by-one. If you shout them out to the stadium, every student does one addition and raises their hand. The stadium finishes in 1 second!

⚡ The 2017 connection

Because GPUs are designed to color millions of pixels at the same time, they do "parallel math". When the 2017 Transformer turned AI into giant grids of simple matrix math, the GPU became the perfect engine for it. That is why NVIDIA became the engine room of the AI era, and why GPUs are now the most fought-over hardware on Earth.

🎮 Try it yourself: The Professor vs. The Stadium

Let's say a computer needs to color a game screen with **2,000,000 pixels**:

  • 🎓 CPU (The Professor): Runs at a super fast speed, taking 1 microsecond per pixel. To do all 2,000,000 pixels one-by-one, it takes 2 seconds.
  • 🏫 GPU (The Stadium): Has 2,000 individual cores. Each core is slower, taking 10 microseconds per pixel. But because they work at the same time, they color 2,000 pixels every 10 microseconds. Total time: only 0.01 seconds!

For painting game graphics or running AI neural networks, doing things in parallel is 200× faster!

How this floor evolved

One unbroken trend: the switch keeps shrinking.

Then

A room of glowing tubes

1945: ENIAC filled a large room, weighed about 30 tons, and used thousands of hot, fragile vacuum tubes that failed often.

Now

Billions on a fingernail

Modern chips hold tens of billions of transistors, sip battery power, and pack data-centre power onto a desktop.

Next

AI-shaped silicon

Chips designed specifically for AI math — and personal AI supercomputers — are now the cutting edge, with quantum and brain-inspired chips on the horizon.

1945
ENIAC — the first general-purpose electronic computer ↗

Completed in 1945 at the University of Pennsylvania for the US Army, ENIAC could do about 5,000 additions a second using ~17,000 vacuum tubes. It proved a machine could compute at electronic speed.
1947
The transistor is invented ↗

At Bell Labs, Bardeen, Brattain and Shockley build the first transistor — the solid-state switch that replaced the vacuum tube and made everything after it possible.
1958
The integrated circuit — escaping the "tyranny of numbers" ↗

Jack Kilby (1958) and Robert Noyce (1959) independently print several transistors onto one chip, eliminating hand-soldered wiring bottlenecks.
1959
IBM 7090 — computing goes transistorised ↗

One of the first fully transistorised commercial mainframes (a transistor-based successor to IBM's tube machines). Far faster and more reliable, it ran scientific work for organisations including NASA.
1963
CMOS is patented — low-power silicon ↗

Frank Wanlass patents Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor (CMOS) logic. By using almost zero power when idle, it made battery-powered laptops and phones possible.
1971
Intel 4004 — the first microprocessor ↗

A whole CPU on one chip with ~2,300 transistors. It put computing on a path off the data-centre floor and into everyday devices.
1980
VLSI Design — chips designed by code ↗

Mead and Conway publish 'Introduction to VLSI Systems', introducing standard digital design rules that let software engineers design chips with millions of transistors.
2025
NVIDIA DGX Blackwell — a personal AI supercomputer ↗

NVIDIA ships Blackwell-based desktop clusters, putting data-centre-grade parallel matrix math power directly at local scale. The cascade made physical.

⚡ This floor's role in the 2017 cascade

Floor 2 is where the cascade became visible and expensive. The moment Floor 1's new math demanded parallel hardware, the GPU went from a gaming part to the most valuable silicon in the world. Whole companies re-oriented around it; new chips and personal AI supercomputers appeared; and value flowed back down to the hardware floor so strongly that, in April 2026, Apple chose a hardware engineer, John Ternus, as its next CEO. In the age of AI, the chip is the kingdom.
