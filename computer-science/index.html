🏢 Why think of it as a tower?

When people say "I want to learn computer science," they usually picture one thing: writing code. But code is just one floor of a much taller building. Below it sit the operating system, the physical chip, the mathematics of what a machine can even compute. Above it sit the network, the intelligence, and finally the screen you actually touch.

The tower has one strict rule: every floor stands on the floor beneath it. An app can't run without software; software can't run without an operating system; an OS can't run without a chip; a chip can't be designed without the mathematics of logic. So when something changes on a lower floor, every floor above it feels it. That single rule is the whole story of this guide — and the reason the year 2017 matters so much.

⚡ The whole argument in five sentences

In 1945, ENIAC proved a machine could compute. For seventy years after that, progress mostly moved up the tower — faster chips, better operating systems, the internet, smarter software.

Then in 2017, eight researchers at Google published a paper called "Attention Is All You Need." It was not a faster chip or a new app. It was a new piece of mathematics and algorithm — a change on Floor 1, the bedrock.

Because Floor 1 sits under everything, that one change rippled upward through all seven floors: it demanded new hardware (Floor 2), new ways to run computers in parallel (Floors 3–4), rewrote how we build software (Floor 5), created the modern AI floor (Floor 6), and changed every app you touch (Floor 7).

That is why this AI moment is different from every hype cycle before it — and why NVIDIA now sells personal AI supercomputers, and Apple, in April 2026, chose a hardware engineer as its next CEO. See the full cascade →
