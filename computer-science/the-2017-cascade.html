The 2017 cascade

How a single paper — not a chip, not an app, but a piece of mathematics — landed on the bottom floor of computing and forced every floor above it to be rebuilt. This is the reason the post-2017 AI moment is different in kind, not just in degree.

🏢 First, the rule that makes this work

The tower has one law: every floor stands on the floor beneath it. Apps need software, software needs an operating system, the OS needs a chip, the chip is designed from mathematics. So a change near the top stays local — a new app changes one app. But a change at the bottom has nowhere to go but up, through everything.

For most of computing's history, the big news happened on the middle and upper floors: faster processors, the personal computer, the internet, the smartphone. The foundation — the math of how we compute — barely moved. That is exactly why 2017 was a shock to the system.

What actually happened in 2017

In plain language, with the real terms.

2017
"Attention Is All You Need" — Vaswani et al., Google

Eight researchers introduced a new design for neural networks called the TransformerThe neural-network architecture introduced in 2017. It processes a whole sequence at once using "attention," instead of reading it one step at a time. Every modern large language model — GPT, Claude, Gemini — is a Transformer.. Its key trick is self-attention: instead of reading a sentence one word at a time, the model looks at all the words at once and learns which ones matter to each other.

🧮 Why it was a Floor 1 change, not a Floor 6 one

Before 2017, the best language models were recurrent networksRNNs / LSTMs — networks that read data one step at a time, passing a memory forward. Powerful, but inherently sequential: step 100 can't start until step 99 finishes. that read text sequentially — word 100 had to wait for word 99. That is a mathematical bottleneck: sequential work cannot be split across thousands of processors.

Attention replaced that sequence with a pile of matrix multiplications — pure linear algebra that can all be done at the same time. That is the heart of it: the Transformer is a new algorithm and mathematical structure (Floor 1) that happens to be almost perfectly parallel. Everything that followed flows from that one property.

🔎 The detail that changed the world

"Parallel" means the work can be cut into thousands of identical pieces and run simultaneously. There already existed a chip built to do exactly that — thousands of small math units working in lockstep: the GPU. In 2017, the new math on Floor 1 suddenly fit the hardware on Floor 2 like a key in a lock. That match is why the cascade was so violent and so fast.

The ripple, floor by floor

One change at the bottom. Watch it climb. (Reading bottom → top.)

Floor 1 Theory & Mathematics — the source

A new algorithm (self-attention) reframes "understanding a sequence" as massively parallel linear algebra. The bedrock shifts.

Floor 2 Hardware — demand explodes

Parallel math means GPUs become the most valuable silicon on Earth. NVIDIA pivots its whole company to AI; new chips (Blackwell) and personal AI supercomputers (DGX Spark/Station) appear.

Floor 3 Architecture & OS — reorganised around parallelism

Computers are now designed around feeding thousands of GPU cores: high-bandwidth memory, CUDA, schedulers that keep accelerators saturated instead of CPUs.

Floor 4 Networking & Cloud — training goes planetary

A single model is trained across thousands of GPUs wired together. Data-centre networking and the cloud are rebuilt to move enormous gradients between machines.

Floor 5 Data & Software — a new way to build

Instead of writing rules by hand, engineers now assemble data and train models. "Software 2.0": the program is learned, not coded line by line.

Floor 6 Artificial Intelligence — reborn

Scaling Transformers produces Large Language Models — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. The entire modern AI field is, quite literally, this one architecture made bigger.

Floor 7 Humans & Apps — everything you touch

Search, writing, coding, image generation, voice assistants — the apps in your hand are re-shaped around models that did not exist before 2017.

One pebble dropped on Floor 1. Seven floors of waves.

You can see the cascade in the real world

If the bedrock really shifted, the heaviest objects in the industry should have moved. They did.

🟩 NVIDIA: a graphics company became the engine room of AI

NVIDIA made chips for video games. Those chips happened to be the parallel hardware the Transformer needed — so NVIDIA became one of the most valuable companies in the world. In October 2025 it began shipping the DGX Spark (and its larger sibling, the DGX Station): a personal AI supercomputer the size of a small box, built on the Grace Blackwell chip, for about $3,999. A machine that would have filled a data centre a decade ago now sits on a desk — because Floor 1 demanded it.

🍎 Apple: the next CEO is a hardware engineer

For decades the prized skill at the top of tech was operations or design. But when intelligence depends on silicon, the most valuable knowledge moves back down to Floor 2. In April 2026, Apple announced that Tim Cook will become Executive Chairman and that John Ternus — its head of Hardware Engineering — will become CEO. The world's most valuable company handing its top job to a hardware engineer is the cascade made visible: in the age of AI, whoever controls the chip controls the future.

📌 The pattern

NVIDIA selling desktop supercomputers and Apple promoting a hardware engineer are not two separate news stories. They are the same story — value flowing back down to the hardware floor because the math on Floor 1 changed what hardware is for.

⚖️ Why this is different from every past "tech hype"

Plenty of trends touched a single floor — a hot programming language (Floor 5), a flashy app category (Floor 7), a faster phone (Floor 2). They came and went because a one-floor change can't move the building.

2017 was a Floor 1 change. The only other shifts of that depth are the founding ideas of computing itself — Turing's 1936 model of computation, the transistor, the microprocessor. Those didn't fade; they became the permanent floor everything else was built on. A Floor 1 change isn't a wave you ride. It's new ground you stand on.
